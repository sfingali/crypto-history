# The Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge

The **Royal Society** is the world's oldest continuously operating scientific academy, founded in 1660 with [John Wilkins](entities/john-wilkins.md) as one of its principal founders and first secretary. It became the institutional embodiment of the "new philosophy" — experimental, collaborative, and empirical inquiry into nature — that marked the Scientific Revolution.

## Origins: The Oxford Circle at Wadham College

The Royal Society did not spring from nothing. Its nucleus formed at **Wadham College, Oxford**, where John Wilkins served as Warden from 1648 to 1659. During this period, Wilkins gathered around him a remarkable community of experimental philosophers:

- **Robert Boyle** — chemist, physicist, author of *The Sceptical Chymist*
- **Christopher Wren** — astronomer, mathematician, future architect of St Paul's
- **Thomas Willis** — physician, pioneer of neurology
- **Robert Hooke** — Wilkins's assistant, future author of *Micrographia*
- **John Wallis** — mathematician, cryptographer for Parliament
- **William Petty** — political economist and surveyor
- **Seth Ward** — astronomer, Savilian Professor of Astronomy

This circle — sometimes called the "Oxford Philosophical Club" or "Experimental Philosophy Club" — met regularly at Wilkins's lodgings in Wadham to conduct experiments, discuss natural philosophy, and read correspondence from scholars across Europe. Contemporaries described Wadham under Wilkins as a place where "the new philosophy" was freely and vigorously pursued.

## Foundation (1660)

The Restoration of Charles II in 1660 brought the Oxford circle (many of whom had relocated to London) together with another philosophical group centred on Gresham College. On **28 November 1660**, after a lecture by Wren at Gresham, twelve men — including Wilkins, Boyle, Wren, and Petty — agreed to form "a Colledge for the Promoting of Physico-Mathematicall Experimentall Learning."

The Society received its Royal Charter from Charles II on **15 July 1662**, becoming "The Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge." Wilkins was instrumental in drafting its founding documents and served as one of its **two first secretaries** (alongside Henry Oldenburg), responsible for managing its correspondence, recording its proceedings, and editing its *Philosophical Transactions* — the world's first scientific journal.

## Wilkins's Role

Wilkins was more than a founder — he was the "principal reviver of experimental philosophy" and the driving spirit of the early Society. His 1641 book [*Mercury*](entities/mercury-wilkins.md) had already demonstrated his encyclopaedic, evidence-based approach to knowledge. John Aubrey described him as "a very ingeniose man, and had a very mechanical head; he was much for the advancement of universal learning."

His 1668 [*Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language*](concepts/universal-language-project.md) was published under the Society's sponsorship and dedicated to its president — the project of classifying all knowledge into a systematic framework perfectly embodied the Society's ambition.

## Ethos: The Experimental Method

The Royal Society's motto — ***Nullius in verba*** ("Take nobody's word for it") — encapsulated its rejection of scholastic authority in favour of direct observation and experiment. The Society was committed to:

- **Experiment over authority**: No Aristotle, no Galen — verify by direct trial
- **Collective witnessing**: Experiments performed before witnesses; results recorded and shared
- **Plain style**: Reports in clear, concise English, free of rhetorical flourish
- **Correspondence**: A network of foreign members and correspondents across Europe
- **Utility**: Knowledge in service of practical improvements — navigation, agriculture, industry

This ethos pervades *Mercury*, where Wilkins subjects every claim — from sympathetic magnets to angelic messengers — to empirical scrutiny.

## Prehistory: The Invisible College

Before the Wadham circle, during the Civil War period, the "**Invisible College**" — a loose network of natural philosophers communicating by letter — served as a precursor. Boyle coined the term. It included many of the same figures who would later formalise into the Royal Society. The correspondence networks of the Invisible College (managed in part by Samuel Hartlib) provided the model for the Society's later pan-European correspondence.

## Connection to *Mercury*

*Mercury* (1641) predates the Royal Society by two decades, but it already exhibits the Society's intellectual character:

- **Empirical**: Wilkins tests claims (the magnetic needle, invisible inks) against observation
- **Encyclopaedic**: He catalogues all known methods systematically
- **Plain-styled**: He writes accessible English, not Latin scholasticism
- **Practical**: He addresses real-world needs — private communication, swift messaging
- **Collaborative**: He draws on sources from classical antiquity to his own contemporaries

The preface to *Mercury* also shows Wilkins's typical open-mindedness: he was prepared to investigate [Godwin's](entities/francis-godwin.md) claims seriously, even when they seemed "altogether fabulous."

## Legacy

The Royal Society's institutional model — a chartered body of scientists meeting to present experiments, publish findings, and correspond internationally — became the template for scientific academies across Europe: the Académie des Sciences (Paris, 1666), the Berlin Academy (1700, with Leibniz as first president), and the St Petersburg Academy (1724).

Wilkins's vision of science as a collaborative, experimental, and practical enterprise — rooted in his Wadham years and embodied in the Society — transformed how knowledge was made. *Mercury* was its precursor; the Royal Society was its permanent home.
