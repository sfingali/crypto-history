# Mercury, or The Secret and Swift Messenger

**Full title:** *Mercury: or the Secret and Swift Messenger. Shewing, how a man may with privacy and speed communicate his thoughts to a friend at any distance.*

**Author:** John Wilkins (1614–1672), Bishop of Chester, founder and first secretary of the Royal Society, brother-in-law of Oliver Cromwell.

**First published:** 1641 (London, printed by I. Norton for John Maynard and Timothy Wilkins). Second edition same year (author revealed). Third edition 1694 as part of *The Mathematical Works of John Wilkins*.

**Significance:** The **first book on cryptology written in English**. It introduced the words "cryptographia" (secret writing), "cryptologia" (secrecy in speech), and "cryptomeneses" (the art of secret communication, in general) into the English language. It is also a pivotal work in the history of linguistics, anticipating Wilkins's later universal language project *An Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language* (1668).

## Historical Context

Published in 1641, on the very eve of the English Civil War, *Mercury* appeared at a moment when private communication was both politically charged and practically essential. Wilkins was chaplain to George, Lord Berkeley (to whom the book is dedicated), and later married Oliver Cromwell's sister Robina, making him a figure at the very centre of the revolutionary period's power structures.

The book was partly inspired by Francis Godwin's *The Man in the Moone* (published posthumously in 1638, written 1620s), which featured a system of long-distance communication by smoke and fire signals. Godwin had also published *Nuncius Inanimatus* (1629), a Latin tract on signalling that Wilkins credits as "first occasioning this Discourse." Where Godwin teased with obscurity, however, Wilkins aimed at comprehensive clarity, producing a richly illustrated compendium of every cryptographic method he could find across ancient and modern sources.

## What the Book Covers

*Mercury* is structured around three goals for communication: **secrecy**, **swiftness**, and the **joining of secrecy with swiftness**. It is far broader than a modern cryptography textbook — it encompasses everything from invisible ink to the possibility of communicating with angels.

### Part 1 — Secrecy (Chapters 1–14)

The longest section, covering every known method of concealing messages:

**Cryptologia — secrecy in speech (Chapter 3):**
- Inversion (spelling backwards)
- Transmutation (substitution)
- Diminution (leaving out syllables)
- Augmentation (adding letters)

**Cryptographia — secrecy in writing (Chapters 5–13):**
- *In the materials* (Chapter 5): scytales (wrapping text around a rod), secret inks, knotted strings, strings threaded through holes
- *In form — with common letters*:
  - Transposition ciphers (Chapter 6)
  - Substitution ciphers (Chapter 7): ATBASH, Caesar cipher, keyword-based alphabetic switching
  - "Trevanion's cipher" principle (Chapter 8)
- *In form — with invented characters*:
  - Geometrical ciphers: dots, lines, triangles representing letters; frequency analysis for deciphering (Chapter 11)
  - Stenography / shorthand (Chapter 12)
  - Hieroglyphics and emblems (Chapter 12)
  - **Universal characters** (Chapter 13) — here Wilkins first outlined the idea that would become his life's work: a philosophical language in which symbols represent things directly, not spoken words, allowing all nations to communicate without translation

**Semaeologia — signs and gestures (Chapter 14):**
Wilkins catalogued sign language and gesture systems, distinguishing between "ex congruo" (gestures that resemble what they express) and "ex placito" (arbitrary signs established by mutual agreement). This chapter has made *Mercury* a source text in the history of deaf communication.

### Part 2 — Swiftness (Chapters 15–17)

Methods for speed of communication, from the spiritual to the corporeal:
- By angels or spirits (Chapter 15)
- By inanimate bodies: arrows, bullets (Chapter 16)
- By animate bodies: men, beasts, birds — including carrier pigeons (Chapter 16)

### Part 3 — Joining Secrecy and Swiftness (Chapters 18–20)

How to make communication both fast and private:
- **"Fabulous"** (Chapter 19): the supposed sympathy of magnets — the idea that two magnetised needles would move in unison at any distance
- **"Magical"** (Chapter 19): projecting letters by mirror, enchanted glasses that answer any enquiry
- **"Natural and true"** (Chapter 20): fire and smoke signals; **Polybius's coordinate cipher** (a 5×5 grid where each letter is signalled by two torches — a system still studied in cryptography); variations using bells, flags, and cannons

## Famous Episode: Cracking Godwin's Lunar Code

In Godwin's *The Man in the Moone*, the lunar inhabitants communicate through music. Godwin printed musical staves representing their speech. Wilkins reproduced the same musical notation and demonstrated that it was a simple alphabetic cipher: pitch and duration mapped to letters. Once cracked, the lunar language was revealed to be Latin.

This is among the earliest literary examples of a cryptographic puzzle embedded in fiction, and the earliest crack of one.

## Relationship to Wilkins's Larger Project

*Mercury* was the third of Wilkins's early scientific works, following *The Discovery of a World in the Moone* (1638, a defence of Copernican astronomy and speculation about lunar travel) and *Mathematical Magick* (1648, on mechanical devices). All three were reprinted together in 1694 as *The Mathematical Works of John Wilkins*.

But *Mercury* is most significant as the seed of Wilkins's masterpiece: *An Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language* (1668). In Chapter 13 of *Mercury*, Wilkins first articulated the dream of a universal character — a system of writing where each symbol represents a thing or concept directly, bypassing spoken language entirely, so that all nations could read the same text. This occupied him for the rest of his life and represents one of the most ambitious linguistic projects of the 17th century, influencing Leibniz, Dalgarno, and generations of language planners.

## Cryptographic Significance

Though *Mercury* is the first English-language cryptology text, it is notably *not* a manual for practising cryptographers. While 17th-century diplomats and spymasters (including those in the very Civil War about to erupt) used numerical ciphers (letters or words encoded as two- or three-digit numbers), Wilkins never mentions them. His treatment is encyclopaedic and theoretical rather than practical, drawing from classical sources (Polybius, Caesar, Hebrew scholars on ATBASH) and Renaissance curiosity rather than from the operational cipher methods of his own time.

Nevertheless, two cipher principles described in *Mercury* did see real-world use in the 17th century — "Trevanion's cipher" (embedding a message by reading only certain letters in a text) and "Argyll's cipher" (a transposition method) — though there is no direct evidence the practitioners took them from Wilkins.
