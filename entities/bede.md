# The Venerable Bede (c. 673–735)

**The Venerable Bede** was an Anglo-Saxon monk, historian, and scholar at the monastery of Jarrow in Northumbria, best known for his *Ecclesiastical History of the English People* (731). But in [[mercury-wilkins|*Mercury*]], [[john-wilkins|Wilkins]] references Bede for a more obscure contribution: his treatise *De Loquela per Gestum Digitorum* ("On Speaking Through the Gestures of the Fingers"), which made Bede an early authority on **dactylology** — finger-signing — and secured his place in the history of sign language and secret communication.

## Life and Works

Born around 673 near the monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow, Bede spent virtually his entire life within its walls. He was one of the most learned men of his age, producing commentaries on scripture, works on chronology (he popularised the AD/BC dating system), scientific treatises, and the *Ecclesiastical History* that earned him the title "Father of English History."

His scientific works include *De Natura Rerum* (On the Nature of Things), *De Temporibus* (On Time), and *De Temporum Ratione* (On the Reckoning of Time) — and it is within this computational tradition that his finger-signing system appears.

## *De Loquela per Gestum Digitorum*

Also known as *De Computo vel Loquela Digitorum* ("On Reckoning or the Speech of Fingers"), this short treatise describes a system for **representing numbers through finger positions** — a gestural number system that allowed silent communication of quantities.

### The System

Bede's system used both hands:

- **Left hand**: Numbers 1–99
  - 1–9: Fingers curved to the palm in specific patterns
  - 10–90: Different hand postures (thumb touching different finger joints, etc.)
- **Right hand**: Numbers 100–9,000
  - 100–900: Mirroring the left hand's system but on the right
  - 1,000–9,000: Additional postures involving interlocking fingers and other configurations

For example:
- 1 = bending the little finger at the middle joint
- 10 = touching the thumb to the index finger's lower joint
- 100 = the same posture but on the right hand
- 1,000 = interlocking all fingers

This allowed a skilled practitioner to express any number up to 9,999 silently and with one gesture.

### Purpose

The system was not primarily secret communication — it was a **computational aid**. Monks used it for calculating the date of Easter (computus), tracking time, and performing arithmetic during services when speech was forbidden. But its potential for silent communication was obvious.

### Classical Precedents

Bede did not invent the system from scratch. Finger-signing for numbers had a long classical tradition:

- **Classical orators** used finger positions to mark the divisions of a speech
- **Roman merchants** used hand gestures to signal prices across noisy markets
- The Roman author **Quintilian** (1st century CE) discusses finger-numbering in his *Institutio Oratoria*
- **Martianus Capella** (5th century) also described a finger-numbering system

Bede's contribution was to codify and Christianise this classical tradition, embedding it in monastic computational practice. The treatise appears as the first chapter of *De Temporum Ratione*, establishing finger-reckoning as the foundation of chronological calculation.

## Wilkins's Reference (Chapter 14)

In [[ch-14-signs-gestures|Chapter 14]] of *Mercury*, Wilkins introduces the concept of **arthrologia** or **dactylologia** — speaking with the fingers and joints of the hand:

> "Such is that upon the joints and fingers of the hand, commonly stiled *arthrologia*, or *dactylologia*; largely treated of by the venerable Bede, Pierius, and others."

Wilkins treats Bede as the primary authority on the tradition of finger-signing, alongside **Pierius Valerianus** (a 16th-century scholar who expanded the system to include letters as well as numbers). Wilkins then develops his own practical finger alphabet, assigning vowels to the tops of fingers, consonants to the middle and bottom parts, and so on.

### Why Bede Matters to Wilkins

Bede's significance for Wilkins is threefold:

1. **Historical depth**: Bede provides a medieval Christian precedent for silent gestural communication — important for Wilkins's encyclopaedic method and his argument that secret communication is not a modern or suspicious invention.

2. **Ex placito signification**: Bede's system is a perfect example of "signs by compact" (*ex placito*) — arbitrary but agreed-upon meanings — which Wilkins distinguishes from "natural" gestures (*ex congruo*) like facial expressions.

3. **Bridge to the deaf**: Bede's finger-signing, expanded by Pierius and Wilkins to include letters, becomes a method for communicating with the deaf and dumb — a topic of intense interest to Wilkins, who describes the remarkable "dialogues of gestures" between deaf people.

## Broader Significance

Bede's *De Loquela* is one of the earliest surviving Western texts on systematic sign language. It influenced:

- **Medieval monastic sign language**: Cistercian and Benedictine monasteries developed rich sign vocabularies for silent communication during periods of imposed silence
- **Renaissance dactylology**: Pierius, Bonet, and others extended finger-signing from numbers to letters, creating manual alphabets
- **Modern sign language**: While ASL and BSL are natural languages (not manual codes for spoken languages), the historical chain from Bede through Wilkins to modern deaf education is real

Wilkins's placement of Bede at the foundation of the dactylological tradition in Chapter 14 of *Mercury* makes the book a key source for the intellectual history of sign language and deaf communication.
