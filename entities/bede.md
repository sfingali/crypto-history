# The Venerable Bede (c. 673–735)

**The Venerable Bede** was an Anglo-Saxon monk, historian, and scholar at the monastery of Jarrow in Northumbria, best known for his *Ecclesiastical History of the English People* (731). But in [[mercury-wilkins|*Mercury*]], [[john-wilkins|Wilkins]] references Bede for a more obscure contribution: his treatise *De Loquela per Gestum Digitorum* ("On Speaking Through the Gestures of the Fingers"), which made Bede an early authority on **dactylology** — finger-signing — and secured his place in the history of sign language and secret communication.

## Life and Works

Born around 673 near the monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow, Bede spent virtually his entire life within its walls. He was one of the most learned men of his age, producing commentaries on scripture, works on chronology (he popularised the AD/BC dating system), scientific treatises, and the *Ecclesiastical History* that earned him the title "Father of English History."

His scientific works include *De Natura Rerum* (On the Nature of Things), *De Temporibus* (On Time), and *De Temporum Ratione* (On the Reckoning of Time) — his masterwork on chronology and the calendar, written in 725. It is within this computational tradition that his finger-signing system appears.

## *De Loquela per Gestum Digitorum*

Also known as *De Computo vel Loquela Digitorum* ("On Reckoning or the Speech of Fingers"), this short treatise is the **first chapter** of *De Temporum Ratione*. Bede placed it at the very beginning of his chronological masterwork, establishing finger-reckoning as the **foundation of all time-calculation**. The treatise describes a system for representing numbers through finger positions — a gestural number system that allowed silent communication of quantities.

### How the System Worked in Practice

Bede's system was a **positional encoding of numbers** using both hands, with each hand responsible for a different range:

- **Left hand**: Numbers 1–99
  - **1–9**: Specific finger-curling patterns on the left hand. For instance, 1 was signalled by bending the little finger at the middle joint; 2 by bending both the little and ring fingers; 3 by bending the little, ring, and middle fingers. Each number had a unique, recognisable gesture.
  - **10–90**: Hand postures involving the thumb touching different finger joints. For example, 10 was signed by touching the thumb to the lower joint of the index finger; 20 by touching the thumb to the middle joint of the index finger; 30 by joining the tips of thumb and index finger in a circle. The system extended through 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, and 90, each with a distinct configuration.

- **Right hand**: Numbers 100–9,000
  - **100–900**: Mirroring the left hand's 1–9 and 10–90 systems, but on the right hand. The same gesture that meant "1" on the left hand meant "100" on the right; "10" on the left became "1,000" on the right.
  - **1,000–9,000**: Additional postures involving the body. For instance, 1,000 could be signed by clasping the hands together with fingers interlocked. Higher thousands involved touching fingers to specific points on the chest, belly, or thighs — extending the signing space from the hands alone to the whole body.

This allowed a skilled practitioner to express **any number up to 9,999** silently and with one or two gestures. The system was efficient enough that monks could conduct complex arithmetic during services without speaking a word.

### Practical Examples from Bede's Text

Bede provides specific instructions. To sign the number 7,000:
- The left hand touches the navel with its middle three fingers extended
- 8,000: the left hand touches the chest with all four fingers extended
- 9,000: the left hand touches the throat with the index finger

For compound numbers, the two hands could work together. To sign 153: the right hand makes the gesture for 100, the left hand makes the gesture for 50, and then the left hand makes the gesture for 3 — all in rapid sequence.

### Purpose: Computus, Not Secrecy

The system was not primarily secret communication — it was a **computational aid**. Monks used it for:

1. **Calculating the date of Easter** (*computus*): The most complex calendrical problem of the medieval Church. Easter depends on both the lunar cycle and the solar year; its calculation required tracking the golden number, the epact, the dominical letter, and other variables — all of which could be worked out on the fingers during the liturgy.

2. **Performing arithmetic during services**: When speech was forbidden (during the Canon of the Mass, for example), monks could still work through computational problems silently.

3. **Teaching arithmetic to novices**: The finger system made abstract number concepts tangible. A novice could literally see that 3 + 4 = 7 by watching the finger configurations.

4. **Timekeeping**: Monks tracked the canonical hours, the lunar phases, and the seasons using finger-reckoning as a portable calculator.

### Classical Precedents

Bede did not invent the system from scratch. Finger-signing for numbers had a long classical tradition:

- **Classical orators** used finger positions to mark the divisions of a speech (*partitio*)
- **Roman merchants** used hand gestures to signal prices across noisy marketplaces
- The Roman rhetorician **Quintilian** (1st century CE) discusses finger-numbering in his *Institutio Oratoria* (Book 1, Chapter 10), noting that an orator who fumbles his finger-counting looks foolish
- **Martianus Capella** (5th century) described a similar finger-numbering system in *De Nuptiis Philologiae et Mercurii*
- The system appears in Greek sources as well: **Nicomachus of Gerasa** (2nd century) and other mathematical writers describe finger-reckoning

Bede's contribution was to **codify and Christianise** this classical tradition, embedding it in monastic computational practice. By placing his finger-reckoning treatise as the opening chapter of *De Temporum Ratione*, he established gestural arithmetic as the foundation of all chronological science — a prestigious position that guaranteed the text's survival and influence.

## Bede's Place in the History of Computus

The *computus* — the calculation of the date of Easter — was the most intellectually demanding routine task of the medieval Church. Getting it wrong meant celebrating the central feast of the Christian year on the wrong day, a scandal that could involve kings, bishops, and papal legates. The Synod of Whitby (664 CE), which Bede describes in his *Ecclesiastical History*, was largely a debate over competing Easter-calculation methods.

Bede's *De Temporum Ratione* was the definitive solution. It synthesised:
- **Solar calendar**: The Julian year of 365.25 days
- **Lunar calendar**: The 19-year Metonic cycle (after which lunar phases repeat on the same calendar dates)
- **Paschal tables**: The 532-year cycle after which the date of Easter repeats exactly

Bede's finger-reckoning treatise was the practical tool that made this complex system usable. A monk working through the *computus* tables could track the golden number (year in the 19-year lunar cycle) on his left hand while cross-referencing the dominical letter (year in the 28-year solar cycle) on his right. The two-hand design of Bede's system — left hand for 1–99, right hand for 100–9,000 — was not arbitrary but perfectly adapted to the dual-cycle structure of the *computus*.

## Wilkins's Reference (Chapter 14)

In [[ch-14-signs-gestures|Chapter 14]] of *Mercury*, Wilkins introduces the concept of *ex placito* signs — gestures whose meaning derives not from natural resemblance but from "use and mutual compact":

> "But some there are of more especial note for their use and antiquity. Such is that upon the joints and fingers of the hand, commonly stiled *arthrologia*, or *dactylologia*; largely treated of by the venerable Bede, Pierius, and others."

The footnote in the 1802 text identifies Bede's work as: *lib. de loquela per gestum digitorum sive de indigitatione* ("Book on speech through the gesture of the fingers, or on finger-pointing").

Wilkins then describes the system in his own words:

> "In whom you may see, how the ancients were wont to express any number by the several postures of the hands and fingers: the numbers under a hundred, were denoted by the left hand, and those above, by the right hand. Hence Juvenal, commending Rylias for his old age, says, that he reckoned his years upon his right hand."

The Juvenal quotation (*Satire 10*) — *"Felix nimirum qui tot per saecula vitam / Distulit, atque suos jam dextra computat annos"* ("Happy indeed is he who has delayed death through so many ages, and now counts his years on his right hand") — confirms that finger-reckoning was a familiar cultural reference in classical Rome, not just a monastic specialty.

### Why Bede Matters to Wilkins

Bede's significance for Wilkins is threefold:

1. **Historical depth**: Bede provides a medieval Christian precedent for silent gestural communication — essential for Wilkins's encyclopaedic method and his argument that secret communication is not a modern or suspicious invention. If the Venerable Bede, the most respected scholar of the Anglo-Saxon Church, treated finger-signing as a legitimate art, it could hardly be dismissed as sorcery or subversion.

2. ***Ex placito* signification**: Bede's system is a perfect example of "signs by compact" (*ex placito*) — arbitrary but agreed-upon meanings — which Wilkins distinguishes from "natural" gestures (*ex congruo*) like facial expressions. The distinction is crucial to Wilkins's larger argument that communication systems are **constructed**, not given — and therefore can be optimised, extended, and adapted for secrecy.

3. **Bridge to the deaf**: From Bede's number-signing, Wilkins proceeds to his own **finger alphabet** — a system for spelling out words through finger positions. He assigns vowels to the tops of the fingers, consonants to the middle and bottom parts, with additional consonants mapped to the spaces between fingers, the wrist, and the centre of the palm. This transforms Bede's *numerical* system into a *literal* one — a complete manual alphabet. Wilkins then connects this directly to communication with "such as are born both deaf and dumb," describing the remarkable "dialogues of gestures" that pass between deaf people who have never heard a spoken word.

### The Finger Alphabet: Wilkins's Extension

After describing Bede's system, Wilkins offers his own practical finger alphabet:

> "As for example: let the tops of the fingers signify the five vowels; the middle parts, the five first consonants; the bottoms of them, the five next consonants; the spaces betwixt the fingers the four next. One finger laid on the side of the hand may signify T, two fingers V the consonant, three W, the little finger crossed X, the wrist Y, the middle of the hand Z."

This maps the entire alphabet onto a single hand — a system a confederate could use silently, in plain sight, without arousing suspicion (Wilkins notes that excessive gesturing "will not be without suspicion" and recommends embedding the signs in "actions of more common unsuspected use" like scratching the head, rubbing the face, or twisting the beard).

The progression from Bede's number system to Wilkins's letter system is the progression from **computation to communication** — from counting to spelling, from the monastery to the spy's tradecraft.

## Connection to Later Monastic Sign Language Traditions

Bede's finger-reckoning treatise was not the beginning of monastic sign language, but it was an important link in a chain that extended for centuries:

### The Cluniac Sign Lexicon

The **Cluniac order** (based at the Abbey of Cluny, founded 910) developed one of the most elaborate monastic sign languages. The *Consuetudines Cluniacenses* (customs of Cluny), compiled under Abbot Hugh I (1049–1109), include extensive sign vocabularies for use during periods of imposed silence. Monks had signs for:
- Liturgical objects (cross, chalice, Gospel book)
- Foods (bread: "make a circle with both thumbs and forefingers"; fish: "imitate the motion of a fish's tail with your hand")
- People (abbot: "touch your head with your finger"; prior: "make the sign for abbot and then hold up one finger")
- Abstract concepts (anger, humility, prayer)

The Cluniac system influenced the **Cistercian** sign lexicons (the Cistercians, founded 1098, broke from Cluny but retained and developed the sign tradition) and spread throughout Benedictine monasteries across Europe.

### From Bede to Cluny

Bede's treatise was known and copied in the centuries between his death and the Cluniac reforms. The manuscript tradition is well-attested: *De Temporum Ratione* was one of the most copied scientific works of the early Middle Ages, and its opening finger-reckoning chapter was transmitted as part of the standard *computus* curriculum. Monastic sign vocabularies developed independently, but Bede's work provided a **theoretical foundation** — a demonstration that systematic gestural communication was a legitimate intellectual tradition with classical and patristic precedent.

### The Renaissance Expansion: Pierius Valerianus

Between Bede and Wilkins stands **Pierius Valerianus** (Giovanni Pietro Bolzani, 1477–1558), the second authority Wilkins names alongside Bede. Valerianus's *Hieroglyphica* (1556) extended finger-signing from numbers to letters, developing a complete manual alphabet. This is the bridge Wilkins crosses: from Bede's numerical dactylology through Valerianus's literal alphabet to his own practical system.

### The Deaf Education Connection

The chain from Bede through monastic sign languages to deaf education is historically significant. In the 16th and 17th centuries, several figures developed manual alphabets for teaching the deaf:
- **Pedro Ponce de León** (1520–1584), a Spanish Benedictine, taught deaf children of noble families using finger-spelling
- **Juan Pablo Bonet** (1573–1633) published *Reducción de las letras y arte para enseñar a hablar a los mudos* (1620), describing a one-handed manual alphabet
- Wilkins cites **Petrus Pontius** (Pedro Ponce de León's successor) and **Valesius** as authorities on teaching the deaf to speak through written characters and lip-reading

Wilkins's interest in the deaf is both practical and philosophical. The deaf person, imprisoned in a body "that had no way at all to express its cogitations," represents the **limit case** of communication — and therefore the test of any communication system. If Bede's finger-signing, expanded into a manual alphabet, can give the deaf a language, it proves the power of *ex placito* signification.

## Broader Significance

Bede's *De Loquela* is one of the earliest surviving Western texts on systematic sign language. Its influence threads through:

- **Medieval monastic sign language**: Cluniac, Cistercian, and Benedictine sign vocabularies for silent communication during periods of imposed silence
- **Renaissance dactylology**: Pierius, Bonet, and others extended finger-signing from numbers to letters, creating full manual alphabets
- **Modern sign language**: While ASL and BSL are natural languages distinct from manual codes for spoken languages, the historical chain from Bede through Wilkins to modern deaf education is genuine
- **Cryptographic gesture systems**: Wilkins's adaptation of Bede's system for secret communication establishes gestural cryptography as a legitimate branch of the art

Wilkins's placement of Bede at the foundation of the dactylological tradition in Chapter 14 of *Mercury* makes the book a key source for the intellectual history of sign language and deaf communication — and demonstrates the breadth of Wilkins's vision, which encompassed not merely ciphers on paper but the full spectrum of human communication, from the whispered word to the signing hand.
