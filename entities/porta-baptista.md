# Giambattista della Porta (1535–1615)

**Giambattista della Porta** (also known as Giovanni Battista della Porta, Baptista Porta) was a Neapolitan polymath — cryptographer, natural philosopher, dramatist, and one of the most cited authorities in [John Wilkins's](entities/john-wilkins.md) [*Mercury*](entities/mercury-wilkins.md). His *Magia Naturalis* (Natural Magic, 1558) and *De Furtivis Literarum Notis* (On Secret Writing, 1563) made him the foremost Renaissance authority on cryptography before Wilkins.

## Life

Born in Naples in 1535 to a noble family, Porta was largely self-educated, devouring books in his family's library and conducting experiments at home. He founded the **Accademia Secretorum Naturae** (Academy of the Secrets of Nature) — possibly the first scientific society — where members presented experimental findings. The Academy was suppressed by the Inquisition around 1578 on suspicion of sorcery, a recurring theme in Porta's career.

He wrote over twenty books on subjects ranging from cryptography to optics, physiognomy, agriculture, hydraulics, and the camera obscura (which he helped develop). He was a pioneering figure in what was then called "natural magic" — the study of nature's hidden powers, on the cusp between medieval occultism and modern experimental science.

## *De Furtivis Literarum Notis* (1563)

Porta's cryptographic treatise *De Furtivis Literarum Notis* ("On the Secret Marks of Letters") was the most comprehensive work on the subject before *Mercury*. Wilkins draws on it throughout *Mercury*, and Porta is frequently grouped with [Trithemius](entities/trithemius.md) and Selenus as the three pre-Wilkins cryptographic authorities.

Key contributions in *De Furtivis*:

- **The cipher disk**: A circular device with two concentric alphabets, one rotatable, allowing rapid encryption and decryption by substitution. This is the "wheel character" that Wilkins references in [Chapter 7](concepts/ch-7-equal-letters-powers.md) — "you may see largely described by Porta."
- **Frequency analysis**: Porta provided systematic rules for breaking substitution ciphers by letter frequency — the method Wilkins expands upon in [Chapter 11](concepts/ch-11-invented-characters.md)
- **Secret inks**: Extensive catalogue of invisible writing methods, cited by Wilkins in [Chapter 5](concepts/ch-5-materials.md)
- **Steganographic methods**: Concealment techniques beyond cryptography

## *Magia Naturalis* (1558, expanded 1589)

*Magia Naturalis*, in twenty books, was a bestseller across Europe. It covered optics, magnetism, chemistry, meteorology, cooking, cosmetics, distillation, and cryptography — all under the banner of "natural magic," which Porta carefully distinguished from demonic magic:

> "Natural Magic is nothing else but the survey of the whole course of Nature."

The book's cryptographic sections included invisible inks, optical tricks for secret communication, and the famous story of the **siege of Navarre** (see below). Wilkins quotes *Magia Naturalis* approvingly, treating Porta as a reliable authority on physical and chemical methods, while rejecting his more credulous claims (like the lamp that burned for centuries in a tomb).

## The Cipher Disk

Porta's most enduring practical invention is the **cipher disk** — a device consisting of two concentric circles, each bearing the alphabet, where the inner disk can rotate relative to the outer. To encode a message, you set the inner disk to a pre-arranged position (the key) and read each plaintext letter on the outer ring, writing the corresponding ciphertext letter from the inner ring.

This is the ancestor of the Confederate cipher disk used in the American Civil War and of the modern mechanical cipher machines (like the Enigma). Wilkins's key-character cipher in Chapter 7 is a conceptual equivalent — a "rotating" alphabet — though Wilkins develops it as a written table rather than a physical device.

## The Siege of Navarre Story

Wilkins relates this dramatic anecdote from Porta:

> When the citizens of Navarre were besieged and their city was on the point of surrendering, a certain man within proposed to walk out through the enemy's camp, passing himself off as a deserter. Before leaving, he swallowed a small box containing a letter written on thin paper. Once through the lines, he recovered the box "amongst other excrements" and delivered the letter to the relieving forces.

Wilkins mentions this in [Chapter 4](concepts/ch-4-secret-conveyances.md) alongside the similar story from [Josephus](entities/josephus.md) about Jews swallowing gold at the siege of Jerusalem. He pairs the two to illustrate the extreme lengths to which people would go for secret communication "for want of skill in this subject that is here discoursed of" — Wilkins's implicit argument that cryptology renders such bodily extremes unnecessary.

## Wilkins's Citations of Porta

Porta appears more often in *Mercury* than almost any other author. Key citations:

| Chapter | Reference |
|---------|-----------|
| Chapter 1 | Porta listed among "modern" authorities on secret communication |
| Chapter 5 | Porta on secret inks and the limits of cryptanalysis |
| Chapter 7 | Porta's cipher disk as "the wheel character" |
| Chapter 8 | Porta's code-book method (with Trithemius and Selenus) |
| Chapter 11 | Porta's frequency analysis rules for breaking ciphers |
| Chapter 19 | Porta's account of a perpetual lamp and other dubious claims |
| Chapter 20 | Porta on optical telegraphy methods |

Wilkins trusts Porta on cryptography but is more sceptical of his "natural magic" — emblematic of the boundary-drawing that defines *Mercury*'s project of separating genuine science from credulity.

## The Accademia Secretorum Naturae

Porta's short-lived academy in Naples may have been the **first scientific society** — predating the [Royal Society](entities/royal-society.md) by nearly a century. Like the Royal Society, it required members to present experimental findings. Unlike it, it was suppressed. The Inquisition's suspicion of Porta (he was also investigated for *Magia Naturalis*) underscores how dangerous "experimental philosophy" could be in Counter-Reformation Italy — and how fortunate Wilkins was to operate in a more tolerant English context.

## Legacy

Porta stands at the transition point between Renaissance natural magic and modern experimental science. His cipher disk is a genuine contribution to cryptographic technology; his *De Furtivis* is the most important pre-Wilkins work on the subject; and his systematic approach to frequency analysis helped establish cryptology as a rational discipline. Wilkins's frequent citation of Porta reflects genuine intellectual debt — *Mercury* builds directly on Porta's foundations.
