# Flavius Josephus (c. 37–c. 100 CE)

**Flavius Josephus** (born Yosef ben Matityahu) was a Jewish historian and military commander whose works — particularly *The Jewish War* (*Bellum Judaicum*) — are among the most important sources for first-century Jewish history. In [[mercury-wilkins|*Mercury*]], [[john-wilkins|John Wilkins]] references Josephus for a single, memorably grotesque anecdote: Jews swallowing gold at the siege of Jerusalem.

## Life

Josephus was born in Jerusalem around 37 CE to a priestly family. When the First Jewish-Roman War broke out in 66 CE, he was appointed military governor of Galilee. After the fall of the fortress at Jotapata, Josephus surrendered to the Romans — famously predicting (he claimed) that the Roman commander Vespasian would become emperor. When this prophecy came true in 69 CE, Josephus was freed and became a Roman citizen, adopting the family name Flavius in honour of his patron.

He spent the rest of his life in Rome, writing histories designed to explain (and justify) the Jewish people and their war to a Roman audience. His major works:

- ***Bellum Judaicum*** (*The Jewish War*, c. 75 CE): An eyewitness account of the war, including the siege and destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE
- ***Antiquitates Judaicae*** (*Jewish Antiquities*, c. 94 CE): A history of the Jewish people from creation to the outbreak of the war
- ***Contra Apionem*** (*Against Apion*): A defence of Judaism against Greek critics
- ***Vita*** (*Life*): An autobiography

## The Swallowed Gold Story

In [[ch-4-secret-conveyances|Chapter 4]] of *Mercury*, Wilkins catalogues every known method of physically concealing a written message. After describing the methods involving animal carcasses, wax tablets, and scalp tattoos, he mentions a particularly extreme example:

> "Some of the fugitive Jews at the siege of Jerusalem did swallow down their gold in little bullets, that afterwards amongst other excrements they might recover it again."

The story comes from Josephus's *Bellum Judaicum*, where it forms part of his harrowing account of the Roman siege of Jerusalem (70 CE). According to Josephus, some Jewish fugitives, fleeing the starving and burning city, melted their gold into small pellets and swallowed them before attempting escape — intending to recover the gold from their bodies later. Josephus reports that the Roman soldiers, having learned of this practice (possibly from Syrian auxiliaries who noticed gold in the excrement of captured Jews), began **disembowelling** captives to search for swallowed treasure.

The story is not about secret communication — it is about secret *transportation* of wealth. But Wilkins uses it to make a larger point:

> "And as it was possible for a man to write Homer's Iliads in a nut-shell... so he might in that huge bulk and capacity, contrive a whole pacquet of letters."

The logic is characteristically Wilkinsian: if people went to such extreme lengths for *gold* "for want of skill" in better methods, imagine what they could do with proper cryptological knowledge. The swallowed-gold story is evidence, in Wilkins's framing, of the desperation caused by the absence of systematic cryptographic technique.

## Wilkins's Broader Use of Josephus

Josephus also appears in Chapter 4 alongside a second anecdote: at the siege of **Jotapata**, Josephus himself (as the defending commander) sent messengers who "crept out of the city by night like dogs" — crawling on all fours past the Roman sentries. This is a complementary example of extreme physical concealment.

The thread connecting both stories is the **ingenuity born of desperation** when normal communication is impossible — the exact problem that cryptography, Wilkins argues, can solve without bodily risk.

## Historical Significance

Josephus's account of the Jewish War is the **only detailed eyewitness source** for the destruction of Jerusalem and the fall of Masada. His reliability is debated — he was, after all, writing for his Roman patrons — but his works remain indispensable. The swallowed-gold episode appears in *Bellum Judaicum* Book 5, in the context of the famine and chaos within Jerusalem during the siege.

For the history of cryptography, Josephus is a minor figure — a source of vivid illustration rather than technical contribution. But his presence in *Mercury* demonstrates Wilkins's thoroughness: he scoured classical historians for any scrap of anecdote that might illuminate the human need for secret conveyance, and his pages are richer for it.

## Connection to Porta's Navarre Story

Wilkins pairs the Josephus swallowed-gold story with [[porta-baptista|Porta's]] account of a man at the siege of Navarre who swallowed a box containing a letter. Both involve ingestion and recovery; both underline the physical extremity of pre-cryptographic secret communication; both serve Wilkins's rhetorical purpose of demonstrating why cryptographic knowledge matters.
