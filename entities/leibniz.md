# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716)

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** was a German polymath — mathematician, philosopher, logician, diplomat, and linguist — whose lifelong project of a *characteristica universalis* (universal characteristic) was directly influenced by [John Wilkins's](entities/john-wilkins.md) work on universal language, particularly [*An Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language*](concepts/universal-language-project.md) (1668).

## Life and Scope

Leibniz was born in Leipzig in 1646, the son of a moral philosophy professor. He studied law and philosophy but ranged across virtually every field of knowledge: he invented calculus independently of Newton (producing a more elegant notation still used today), wrote on metaphysics (monadology), designed calculating machines, reformed library cataloguing, and served as diplomat and historian to the House of Hanover.

He was elected a **Fellow of the [Royal Society](entities/royal-society.md)** in 1673, visiting London that same year. He maintained extensive correspondence with scholars across Europe — a network of letters numbering over 15,000.

## The *Characteristica Universalis*

Leibniz's most ambitious intellectual project was the *characteristica universalis* — a formal language where all concepts would be represented by symbols, and reasoning could be reduced to calculation:

> "If we had it, we should be able to reason in metaphysics and morals in much the same way as in geometry and analysis... If controversies were to arise, there would be no more need of disputation between two philosophers than between two accountants."

This vision shares DNA with Wilkins's *Essay*. Where Wilkins aimed to classify all knowledge into 40 genera with systematic derivative symbols, Leibniz went further: he wanted not just a notation for *things* but a calculus for *reasoning* — a language where you could **calculate** truth.

### How Wilkins's *Essay* (1668) Influenced Leibniz

Leibniz read Wilkins's *Essay* soon after its publication and corresponded about it extensively. His notes record both admiration and criticism:

1. **Classification of knowledge**: Leibniz admired Wilkins's hierarchical tables — the effort to enumerate and systematise all concepts — but found the classification somewhat arbitrary and "insufficiently philosophical." He attempted his own more rigorous classification.

2. **Symbols representing concepts directly**: Wilkins's insight — that written characters could bypass spoken language and represent ideas — is exactly Leibniz's starting point. The Chinese writing system (cited by both) was proof of concept: people who cannot speak each other's languages can read the same text.

3. **The *ars combinatoria***: Where Wilkins sorted concepts into categories, Leibniz aimed to reduce all concepts to a small set of **primitives** from which every other concept could be composed — an "alphabet of human thoughts." This combinatorial approach goes beyond Wilkins.

4. **Pronunciation independence**: Wilkins's observation that a Jew, Greek, Italian, Frenchman, German, and Englishman might read the same character ♉ and each pronounce it differently (שור, Ταυρον, toro, taureau, Stier, "a bull") yet "all agree in the same conceit of them" — this became a cornerstone of Leibniz's own arguments for the *characteristica*.

## Connection to *Mercury* (1641)

Leibniz never mentions *Mercury* directly by name in surviving correspondence, but **Chapter 13** of *Mercury* — where Wilkins first articulates the concept of a universal character — is the logical precursor. The seed that Wilkins planted at age 27 in *Mercury* — "if there were such an universal character to express things and notions, as might be legible to all people and countries" — flowered in both Wilkins's own *Essay* (1668) and in Leibniz's *characteristica universalis*.

Leibniz's relationship with the Royal Society (he was elected FRS in 1673, the same year he demonstrated his calculating machine in London) meant he was well-positioned to encounter Wilkins's ideas through that channel. Wilkins was Bishop of Chester by this time but still active in the Society's affairs.

## Correspondence with Wilkins

Leibniz corresponded with members of Wilkins's circle — particularly **Henry Oldenburg**, the Royal Society's secretary — and through Oldenburg would have been aware of Wilkins's work. The *Essay* was published under the Royal Society's sponsorship and dedicated to its president, making it impossible for any active Fellow like Leibniz to miss.

Leibniz also corresponded with **George Dalgarno**, the Scottish linguist who published *Ars Signorum* (1661) — another universal language project that predated Wilkins's *Essay* and which Wilkins acknowledged in his preface. Leibniz read Dalgarno alongside Wilkins and attempted to synthesise their approaches.

## The Correspondence on Real Characters

In a 1679 letter to Walter von Tschirnhaus, Leibniz directly cited Wilkins: "The late Bishop of Chester's Essay towards a Real Character is a work which deserves to be studied, though I would have wished the characters to be more philosophical." He admired Wilkins's ambition but thought the execution fell short — the symbols were arbitrary rather than logically derived from first principles.

## Legacy

Though neither Wilkins's *Essay* nor Leibniz's *characteristica universalis* succeeded as practical universal languages, their influence extended far beyond linguistics:

- **Leibniz's project** directly inspired Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879), modern predicate logic, and the dream of automated reasoning
- **Wilkins's tables** influenced Linnaeus's taxonomy, Roget's Thesaurus, and Dewey Decimal classification
- The idea of a "concept-based" system recurs in modern semantic web technologies, ontologies, and knowledge graphs

Both men shared the conviction that the confusion of tongues at Babel was a problem to be solved — not by returning to an original Adamic language, but by constructing a new one from reason itself.
