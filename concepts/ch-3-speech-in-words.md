# Chapter 3: Secrecy of Speech in Words

Where Chapter 2 dealt with hiding meaning in the **matter** of discourse (parables, allegory), this chapter treats secrecy in the **words themselves** — altering the form of speech so that it is unintelligible to outsiders.

## Two Approaches

1. **Inventing entirely new words** by compact — a private vocabulary
2. **Altering known language** — transforming ordinary speech into something that "shall seem as obscure, as if it were altogether barbarous"

## Inventing New Words

### The Canting of Beggars

Wilkins identifies beggars' cant as an example: "who though they retain the common particles, yet have imposed new names upon all such matters as may happen to be of greatest consequence and secrecy." This is a practical, living cryptolect.

### The Charms of Witches

> "And of this nature the charms of witches, and language of magicians seem to be. Though of these it may well be doubted, whether they have any signification at all."

Wilkins speculates that the devil himself invented "such horrid and barbarous sounds" to delude his "credulous disciples." He provides extraordinary examples from Martinus de Aries, an archdeacon in Navarre, who found a conjuring book containing these invocations:

> *"Conjuro te per celim, per alion, per seboain, per adamm, per alielujah, per tanti, per archabultir, &c."*

And further: *"Corisciofy, Matatron, Caladafoly, Ozcozoy, Yosiel, &c."*

Wilkins notes shrewdly that "the common particles and words of usual sense are plainly set down in ordinary Latin" — only the apparently efficacious words are gibberish. "The inventions of this kind do not fall under any particular rule or maxim, but may be equally infinite to the variety of articulate sounds."

## Altering a Known Language: Four Methods

### 1. Inversion (Spelling Backwards)

> *"Mitto tibi Metulas cancros imitare legendo"*

Where *Salutem* is expressed by inverting the letters. Or: *"Stisho estad, veca biti"* → *Hostis adest, cave tibi* (by inverting syllables).

### 2. Transmutation

Mutual changing of one letter for another in pronunciation — the spoken equivalent of the substitution ciphers in [Chapter 7](concepts/ch-7-equal-letters-powers.md). Wilkins admits "this may seem of great difficulty, yet use and experience will make it easy."

### 3. Diminution (Contraction)

Leaving part of words out — as in ancient manuscripts where *a a* stood for *anima*, *Ar's* for *Aristoteles*. But Wilkins notes this "can be but of small use in the English tongue, because that does consist most of monosyllables."

### 4. Augmentation (Adding Letters/Doubling Vowels)

The method Wilkins finds most practical for English:

> *"Our plot is discovered"* becomes *"Ougour plogot igis digiscogovegereged"*

By doubling each vowel and inserting a 'G' (or any other consonant: K, P, T, R, etc.). He notes:

> "It is so easy to be learnt, that I have known little children, almost as soon as they could speak, discourse to one another as fast this way, as they could in their plainest English."

This is essentially the children's game of **Pig Latin** or a vowel-doubling cipher — a spoken code that sounds like "barbarous" nonsense but is easily learned and can be spoken rapidly.

## The Grand Inconvenience

> "But all these latter kinds of secrecy in speech have this grand inconvenience in them, that they are not without suspicion."

A person speaking gibberish or inverted words is obviously hiding something — violating the second condition of secrecy (freedom from suspicion) established in Chapter 2. This foreshadows Wilkins's search for methods that are both secure *and* unsuspicious.

## Key Quotes

> "The secret ways of speaking, which consist in the matter of discourse, have been already handled. Those that are in the words are twofold. Either, 1. By inventing new words of our own... 2. Or by such an alteration of any known language..."

> "I have known little children, almost as soon as they could speak, discourse to one another as fast this way, as they could in their plainest English."
