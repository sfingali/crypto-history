# Chapter 14: Semaeologia — Signs and Gestures

The third branch of secret discourse, completing the *Cryptologia—Cryptographia—Semaeologia* triad. This chapter has made *Mercury* a source text in the history of deaf communication and sign language.

## The Two Kinds of Signs

> "The third way of discoursing was by signs and gestures, which (as they are serviceable to this purpose) may be distinguished into such as are significant, either 1. *Ex congruo*, or 2. *Ex placito*."

### Ex Congruo: Natural Resemblance

> "When there is some natural resemblance and affinity betwixt the action done, and the thing to be exprest."

This covers:
- **Emotional expressions**: Joy, anger, fear — where "outward gestures" naturally correspond to "inward passions." Wilkins quotes Ovid: *"Saepe tacens vocem verbaque vultus habet"* (Often a silent face has voice and words)
- **Biblical reference**: Proverbs 6:13 — the scorner "winketh with his eyes, he speaketh with his feet, he teacheth with his fingers"
- **Religious gestures**: Actions prescribed for priests and Levites in the Old Law, and some still used in Christian worship — "by such bodily gestures and signs, we may as well speak unto God as unto men"
- **Civil ceremonies**: Formal gestures in public solemnities that "in themselves are apt to signify the thing for which they are meant"

### The Parabolical Gesture: Thrasybulus's Lesson

A dramatic example of concealed meaning in gesture:

> "Thrasybulus, who being consulted with, how to maintain a tyranny that was newly usurped: he bid the messenger attend him in the field; where with his wand he whipt off those higher ears of corn that did over-top the rest; intimating, that it consisted in cutting off the peers and nobility, who were likely to be most impatient of subjection."

Wilkins calls this "a parabolical way of speaking by gestures" — the gestural equivalent of the parables in Chapter 2.

### Ex Placito: By Compact

> "When these signs have their signification from use and mutual compact."

This includes:
- **Amorous signs**: Ovid's *De Arte Amandi* — *"Verba superciliis sine voce loquentia dicam, / Verba leges digitis..."*
- **Trade between nations**: Men who don't share a language "entertain a mutual commerce and traffic" through gesture

## The Deaf and Dumb: A Remarkable Observation

> "It is a strange thing to behold, what dialogues of gestures there will pass betwixt such as are born both deaf and dumb; who are able by this means alone, to answer and reply unto one another as directly if they had the benefit of speech."

This is an early recognition of natural sign language as a fully capable communication system. Wilkins adds:

> "It were a miserable thing for a rational soul to be imprisoned in such a body as had no way at all to express its cogitations; which would be so in all that are born deaf, if that which nature denied them, were not in this respect supplied by a second nature, custom and use."

### The Turkish Mutes

> "It is a great part of the state and majesty belonging to the Turkish emperor, that he is attended by mutes, with whom he may discourse concerning any private business, which he would not have others to understand."

## Teaching the Deaf to Speak: Pet. Pontius

Wilkins relates a remarkable account from Valesius (*Sacra Philosophia*, chapter 3) about **Pet. Pontius**, a Spanish educator:

> "Who by an unheard-of art taught the deaf to speak. *Docens primum scribere, res ipsas digito indicando, quae characteribus illis significarentur; deinde ad motus linguae, qui characteribus responderent, provocando.*"

Method:
1. First, teach the deaf person to write the name of whatever object is pointed to
2. Then, provoke them "to such motions of the tongue as might answer the several words"

This is the **first documented oral education of the deaf** — predating the better-known work of Juan Pablo Bonet (1620) and Abbé de l'Épée (18th century).

Wilkins speculates that "this invention well followed, might be of singular use for those that stand in need of such helps."

### Gabriel Neale: Lip-Reading

> "Though certainly that was far beyond it, (if true) which is related of an ancient doctor, Gabriel Neale, that he could understand any word by the mere motion of the lips, without any utterance."

## Arthrologia (Dactylology): Speaking with the Fingers

> "Such is that upon the joints and fingers of the hand, commonly stiled *arthrologia*, or *dactylologia*; largely treated of by the venerable Bede, Pierius, and others."

### Numbers on the Hands

The ancients expressed numbers by finger positions:
- Numbers under 100: left hand
- Numbers above 100: right hand

Juvenal commends an old man who "reckoned his years upon his right hand": *"Felix nimirum qui tot per saecula vitam / Distulit, atque suos jam dextra computat annos."*

### Letters on the Hands

> "Hence it is easy to conceive, how the letters as well as the numbers, may be thus applied to the several parts of the hand, so that a man might with divers touches, make up any sense that he hath occasion to discover unto a confederate."

Wilkins provides a practical finger alphabet:
- **Tops of fingers**: the five vowels
- **Middle parts**: the five first consonants
- **Bottoms**: the five next consonants
- **Spaces between fingers**: the four next
- **One finger on side of hand**: T
- **Two fingers**: V (consonant)
- **Three fingers**: W
- **Little finger crossed**: X
- **Wrist**: Y
- **Middle of hand**: Z

### Avoiding Suspicion

> "But because such various gesticulations as are required to this, will not be without suspicion, therefore it were better way, to impose significations upon such actions as are of more common unsuspected use."

Wilkins suggests: scratching the head, rubbing parts of the face, winking eyes, twisting the beard — "any of which, or all of them together, may be as well contrived to serve for this purpose, and with much more secrecy."

### A Wry Closing

> "In which art, if our gaming cheats, and popish miracle-impostors, were but well versed, it might much advantage them, in their cozening trade of life."
