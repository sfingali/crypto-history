# Chapter 17: Secret and Swift Information by the Species of Sound

Having treated of secrecy and swiftness separately, Wilkins now turns to the book's central problem: **joining both together** in the conveyance of any message.

## The Key Principle: Anything Perceptible Can Communicate

> "Whatever is capable of a competent difference, perceptible to any sense, may be a sufficient means whereby to express the cogitations."

This is the chapter's foundational insight. It doesn't matter *what* the medium is — only that it can produce **distinct, perceptible differences**. Two differences alone are enough: transposed through five places, two signs yield 32 combinations — more than the 24 letters of the alphabet require (as explained more fully in Chapter 9).

## Sound as a Medium

Wilkins follows Vegetius's threefold classification of signals:

1. **Vocalia** — articulate sounds (speech)
2. **Semivocalia** — inarticulate sounds (bells, trumpets, drums)
3. **Muta** — visual signs (fire, smoke — treated in Chapter 20)

It's the second category — inarticulate sounds — that concerns him here. Any sound capable of a **double difference** can serve: trumpets, bells, cannons, drums.

Sound signals are slower than light-based signals but "far swifter than the natural motion of any corporeal messenger." Their range is about a mile, but they can be **multiplied** — relayed from station to station across great distances.

## Historical Precedents

### Xerxes's Voice Relay (Cleomenes)
Wilkins relates how Xerxes stationed men at short intervals along the entire route from Persia to Greece, "so near that one could hear the voice of another" — allowing news to travel from Greece to Persia in **forty hours**. Wilkins dismisses this as "too gross for imitation, savouring somewhat of the rudeness of those former and more barbarous ages."

### The Roman Picts Wall (Camden)
A more elegant system: Severus's wall across northern England (over 100 miles), with towers about a mile apart. Between each tower ran **hollow pipes or trunks** built into the wall, through which defenders could inform each other of enemy movements. Since the wall's ruin, locals hold land by "cornage" tenure — bound to blow a horn to warn of enemy incursions.

### Walchius's Voice-Storage Fantasy
Walchius proposed a hollow pipe so contrived that it "shall preserve the voice entirely for certain hours or days." The idea: speak into a sealed pipe, send it to a friend, and when opened, "the words shall come out distinctly, and in the same order wherein they were spoken." From such a contrivance, Walchius claimed, "did Albertus Magnus make his image, and Friar Bacon his brazen head, to utter certain words." Wilkins mentions this also explains "that Popish relic concerning Joseph's *hah*" — the carpenter's grunt preserved in glass.

## The Refutation

Wilkins dismantles the voice-storage idea with physics. Sound propagates "by a kind of continuation and efflux from their first original, as the species of light are from any luminous body." Once separated from their cause, they "presently vanish and die." Trying to trap sound is like trying to "catch the sun-beams, or inclose the light." Both sound and light "have equally the same intrinsical and inseparable dependence upon their efficient causes."

He concedes echoes suggest sound's persistence in air, but compares it to the fiery circle illusion from spinning a fire-stick — neither lasts long enough to be useful.

## The Practical System: Bells and Cannons

None of the historical methods are adequate. Wilkins proposes something better — two bells of different notes, or "some such other audible and loud sounds, which we may command at pleasure, as muskets, cannons, horns, drums."

Using the **binary alphabet** from Chapter 9 (two letters transposed through five places = 32 combinations), any letter can be expressed by a sequence of five sounds. A louder sound represents A, a softer sound represents B.

**Example:** The word "VICTUALS" encoded:
- V = baabb, I = abaaa, C = aaaba, T = baaba, U = baabb
- A = aaaaa, L = ababa, S = baaab

If the sounds have a **triple** difference (three distinct sounds), each letter requires only three signals. With a **quintuple** difference, each letter requires only two — "which will much conduce to the speed and dispatch of such a message."

## The Siege of Navarre (Porta)

Porta relates that when the citizens of Navarre were reduced to extremity and ready to yield, they "discovered to their friends the greatness and kind of their wants, by discharging divers cannons and ordinances in the night-time, according to a certain order before agreed upon; and by this means did obtain such fitting supplies as preserved the city."

## Use Cases

Wilkins imagines scenarios where sound-based secret communication excels:

> "Suppose a friend were perfidiously clapped up in some close dungeon, and that we did not know exactly where, but could only guess at the place, within the latitude of half a mile or somewhat more; a man might very distinctly, by these other inventions, discourse unto him."

> "Or suppose a city were straitly besieged, and there were either within it or without it, such a confederate, with whom we should necessarily confer about some design; we may by these means safely discover to him our intentions."

The messenger employed here "is of so strange a nature, as not to be barred out with walls, or deterred by enemies."
