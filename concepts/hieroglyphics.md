# Hieroglyphics

Egyptian sacred writing, discussed by Wilkins in [Chapter 12](concepts/ch-12-shorthand.md) of *Mercury* as a form of "signifying things and notions" rather than words or letters. The word means "sacred engravings" — symbols carved on pillars, obelisks, pyramids, and other monuments before the invention of alphabetic writing.

Wilkins classifies hieroglyphics alongside **emblems** as a higher form of symbolism, distinct from:
- Characters that signify **letters** (invented alphabets, chapter 11)
- Characters that signify **words** (shorthand, chapter 12)

Hieroglyphics and emblems signify **things and notions directly** — they are conceptual, not linguistic. This makes them a bridge to the [universal character](concepts/ch-13-universal-character.md) that Wilkins dreams of: if symbols can represent concepts rather than sounds, they can be read by anyone regardless of language.

This chapter of *Mercury* was one of the earliest English-language treatments of Egyptian writing as something other than mystical nonsense — Wilkins treats it as a communication system, albeit a limited one.
