# Chapter 18: A Language of Tunes and Musical Notes

This is one of the most original chapters in *Mercury*. Wilkins shows that music itself can be a complete language — an idea sparked by cracking a code embedded in contemporary fiction.

## The Lunar Language Problem

Wilkins's friend Francis Godwin, in his novel *The Man in the Moone* (published posthumously 1638 under the pseudonym Domingo Gonsales), had described the inhabitants of the moon communicating through **musical tones rather than words**. Godwin had even printed musical notation in the book — staves with notes — representing what the lunar people "said."

In his earlier pamphlet *Nuntius Inanimatus* (1629), Godwin had hinted at a system of distant communication without revealing the method. Wilkins's entire book was, as he stated in the preface, "first occasioned" by reading this pamphlet.

## The Musical Alphabet

If the instrument can express notes by **both tone and time** (pitch and duration), then each letter of the alphabet can be represented by a **single sound**. Wilkins reproduces Godwin's code:

- The **five vowels** are represented by minims (half-notes) placed on each of the five lines of the musical stave
- Consonants are encoded by combinations of pitch and note duration
- K and Q are omitted because they "may be otherwise expressed" (C and K are redundant; Q is just K + U)

Using this alphabet, the phrase *Gloria Deo soli* is rendered as a sequence of musical notes — the **words are literally in the tune**.

## The Implications

> "By this you may easily discern how two musicians may discourse with one another, by playing upon their instruments of music, as well as by talking with their instruments of speech."

And "which is a singular curiosity — how the words of a song may be contrived in the tune of it."

That is: a composer could write a melody that, when decoded, spells out a hidden message. The surface is art; the substrate is cryptography.

Wilkins modestly suggests that experts in music theory might improve on his mapping between letters and notes — he's only proposing the idea.

## From Musical Language to Universal Language

The chapter takes a remarkable turn. If these inarticulate sounds encode not letters but **things and notions directly** (as discussed in Chapter 13 on universal characters):

> "Then might there be such a general language, as should be equally speakable by all people and nations; and so we might be restored from the second general curse, which is yet manifested, not only in the confusion of writing, but also of speech."

The **utterance** of musical tunes would serve as the universal language; the **notation** of them as the universal character. Since "all nations do agree in the same conceit of things, so likewise in the same conceit of harmonies," music could transcend Babel.

## Historical Significance

Wilkins claims "this curiosity (for aught I know) has not yet been mentioned by any author" and suggests it deserves "a more full and particular enlargement in a treatise by itself."

This chapter connects three threads that run through Wilkins's entire career:

1. **Cryptography** — the immediate subject of *Mercury*
2. **Universal language** — the seed that became his *Essay towards a Real Character* (1668)
3. **Music as mathematics** — characteristic of the 17th-century scientific worldview

The idea that music could be a **universally intelligible language** — bypassing the confusion of tongues — was genuinely novel. It prefigures later speculative languages like Solresol (François Sudre, 1827), which really was a language built entirely from musical notes.
