# Chapter 8: Secret Writing by More Letters Than Required

Moving from equal-letter ciphers, this chapter covers methods of hiding a message within a larger text — using **more letters than the intended meaning requires**. This is steganography in the purest sense: the secret message is embedded in an innocent-looking cover text.

## Acrostics: Significant First Letters

### The Sibylline Acrostic

> "A writing may be so contrived, that only one letter in a verse shall be significant. As it was in those remarkable acrosticks made by a Sybil concerning our Saviour."

The first letters of each verse spelled out: **ΙΗΣΟΥΣ Χριστος Θεου υιος σωτηρ** — *Jesus Christ the son of God, a Saviour*. Wilkins cites St. Augustine (*De Civitate Dei*, Book 18, chapter 23) and notes the original Greek is preserved by Ludovicus Vives.

### Plautus's Comedies

Plautus hid the names of his comedies in the first letters of their arguments. But "this way is so ordinary in practice, that it needs not any further explication."

## Significant Letters Within Verses

### The Common Distich

> *"Mitto tibi caput Veneris, ventremque Dianae*
> *Latronisque caput, posteriora cani."*

Each word contributes a letter to a hidden message.

## Jewish Notarikon: The Cabalistic Methods

### Capita Dictionum (ראשי תבות)

Letters at the **beginning** of words form a secret message. Wilkins gives examples the rabbis found in Scripture:

- Genesis 49:10 — the capital letters form **ישו** (*Jesu*, Jesus)
- Psalm 72:17 — capital letters similarly form the Messiah's name

### Fines Dictionum (סופי תבות)

Letters at the **end** of words. The classic example: Genesis 1:1 — the final letters of the first words spell **אמת** (*truth*). This "kind of cabalism is six times repeated in the history of the creation. As if Moses by such an artificial contrivance of the letters at the beginning of his writings, did purposely commend unto our belief his following books."

David alludes to this at Psalm 119:160: *"The beginning of thy word is truth."*

Another example: Exodus 3:13-14, where final letters form **יהוה** (*Jehovah*).

### A Warning

Wilkins notes the rabbis "are befooled in their absurd dotage upon these trivial literal collections" — a reason of this nature "is of greater force unto them, than the most evident solid demonstration that may be urged." He mentions Ludovicus Carret, a converted Jewish physician to the French king, who used such arguments to evangelize his former co-religionists because they considered them most effective.

Wilkins also warns: "It were an easy matter for a man that had leisure and patience for such enquiries, to find out sundry arguments of this kind for any purpose" — and that heresies like the Valentinians and Gnostics arose from such freewheeling interpretations.

## The Grille Cipher (Cardan Grille)

> "There is another way of hiding any secret sense under an ordinary epistle, by having a plate with certain holes in it, through which (being laid upon the paper) a man may write those letters or words, that serve to express the inward sense; the other spaces being afterwards filled up with such other words, as in their conjunction to these former, shall contain some common unsuspected business."

This is the **Cardan grille** — attributed to Cardan (in Wilkins's margin note: *Cardan de subtil. l. 17. Porta de furt. l. 2. c. 18*). The secret message is written through the holes, then the gaps are filled with innocent text.

## Trithemius-Porta-Selenus Method

> "There is also another intricate way to this purpose, much insisted on by Trithemius, Porta, and Sylenus. When each usual word or form of an epistle, is varied to as many differences as there are letters, unto which they must all of them be severally assigned."

This is a code-book approach: every letter maps to a different innocuous word or phrase. Wilkins dismisses it — "though they be of great secrecy, yet because they require so much labour and trouble in the writer, I shall therefore pass them over without any further enlargement."

## Key Quotes

> "The inward sense hath likewise been conveyed by some single letters of several words in the same verse."

> "It were an easy matter for a man that had leisure and patience for such enquiries, to find out sundry arguments of this kind for any purpose."
