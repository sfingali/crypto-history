# Chapter 16: Swiftness by Bodies

The practical counterpart to Chapter 15's angels: how physical messengers — animate and inanimate — can achieve speed.

## Inanimate Bodies: Arrows and Bullets

These have "only a violent motion; which cannot therefore be continued to so great a distance" — but for the distance they do cover, they are "far swifter than the natural motion of any animated body." Wilkins refers back to Chapter 4's discussion of arrows and sling-bullets as message carriers.

## Animate Bodies: Men

### Incredible Foot-Speed

- **Lydas**: "so quick in his running, *Ut arenis pendentibus et cavo pulvere, nulla indicia relinqueret vestigiorum*" — left no footprints even on hollow sands
- **A Roman boy** (8 years old): ran 45 miles in an afternoon
- **Anistius and Philonides** (Alexander's footmen): ran 1,200 stadia in a day

Wilkins notes these accounts "will seem less incredible, if we consider the ancient exercises and games of this kind, together with the public fame and rewards for those that were most eminent."

## Beasts

### The Mysterious Ice-Beast

> "Scaliger mentions a story (though he distrusts the truth of it) of a certain beast espied ellead, two of which being joined in a little cart, are said to pass three hundred leagues a day upon the ice."

### Dromedaries, Camels, Mules

Formerly in common use, though in Wilkins's time "the horse (for the most part) serves instead of them all."

### The Post System

> "The custom of riding post, by renewing both horse and man at set stages, is of ancient invention."

Herodotus records that Xerxes used it in the Grecian war; the Persians called it **Αγγαρηιον**. Hermannus Hugo treats the subject extensively in *De Origine Scribendi*, Book 3, chapter 14.

### Wind-Conceived Mares

Pliny told of mares in Lusitania that conceived "merely by the west wind" — no male required. Virgil referenced them in the *Georgics*. Wilkins jokes:

> "Methinks these children of the wind should, for their fleetness, make excellent post-horses, and much conduce to the speedy conveyance of any message."

### Paracelsian Horse Enhancement

The Paracelsians claimed you could "extract the metal and spirit out of one horse, and infuse it into another" — enabling them to "carry a man safely and swiftly through enemies, precipices, or other dangerous places." The wise men of the East supposedly rode such horses at Christ's nativity, or they "had not otherwise been able to have kept pace with a star."

> "If this conceit were feasible, it would much promote the speed of conveyances; but I think it may justly be referred amongst the other dreams of the melancholic chymics."

## Birds: The Swiftest Natural Messengers

> "Amongst all animate bodies, there is not any that have naturally so swift a motion as birds; which if a man could well employ in the dispatch of any errand, there would be but little fear that such messenger should be either intercepted, or corrupted."

### Ancient Examples

- **Volaterranus**: Sent swallows to Rome announcing a victory — "anointed over with the colour of victory"
- **Swallows with knotted strings**: A besieger sent a mother swallow taken from her young back into the city with knotted string showing "after what number of days their aids would come"
- **Hircius and Brutus** (at Mutina): Used pigeons to maintain intelligence between camp and city — "tying their letters unto such pigeons, as were taught beforehand to fly from the tents to the city, and from thence to the tents again"
- **Thaurosthenes**: Sent news of his victory at Olympia to his father in Aegina by pigeon (recorded by Aelian)
- **Anacreon's Ode**: The poet had a pet pigeon he used as a messenger

### Juvenal's Reference

> *"Tanquam e diversis partibus orbis / Anxia praecipiti venisset epistola penna"*

### Roman Magistrates' Pigeons (Varro, via Lipsius)

> "It was usual for the Roman magistrates when they went unto the theatre, or other such public meetings, whence they could not return at pleasure, to carry such a pigeon with them; that if any unexpected business should happen, they might thereby give warning to their friends or families at home."

### Carrier Pigeons in Wilkins's Own Time

> "Nor hath it been less used in these later times, especially in those countries where by reason of continual wars and dissensions, there have been more particular and urgent necessity for such kind of conveyances."

Casaubon: *"Nunc vulgatissima res est, columbas habere ad ejusmodi jussa paratas."* Stewechius: *"Harum opera nostrates hoc hello civili, frequenter adjuti sunt."*

Carrier pigeons were used extensively in the wars of Wilkins's era.

### The Origin of "Carrier"

> "There is a smaller sort of pigeon, of a light body, and swift flight. Which is usually made choice of for such particulars; and therefore the kind of them is commonly called by the name of Carriers."

## Fishes?

> "I doubt not, but that fishes also may be serviceable for this purpose, especially the dolphin, which is reported to be of the greatest swiftness, and most easily circurated, or made tame."
