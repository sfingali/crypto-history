# Emblems

The Renaissance emblem tradition: ornamental designs combining image and text, where the picture carries symbolic meaning beyond the literal subject. Wilkins discusses emblems alongside [hieroglyphics](concepts/hieroglyphics.md) in [Chapter 12](concepts/ch-12-shorthand.md) of *Mercury* as methods of "signifying things and notions."

Emblem books were enormously popular in 16th–17th century Europe — Alciato's *Emblemata* (1531) launched a genre that produced thousands of volumes. An emblem typically consisted of a motto, a picture, and an explanatory verse, with the picture encoding moral, political, or spiritual meanings.

For Wilkins, emblems are interesting because they demonstrate that **visual symbols can carry meaning without words** — the same principle behind the [universal character](concepts/ch-13-universal-character.md) he hopes to create. An emblem speaks across languages because it depicts concepts, not phonemes.

The chapter title page of *Mercury* itself can be read as an emblem: Mercury, the messenger god, carrying secrets swiftly.
