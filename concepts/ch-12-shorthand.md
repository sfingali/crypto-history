# Chapter 12: Shorthand, Hieroglyphics, and Emblems

Wilkins moves from characters that represent letters to characters that represent **words** (stenography/shorthand) and **things/notions** (hieroglyphics and emblems). This chapter bridges cryptography with the broader history of symbolic communication.

## Stenography (Shorthand): Characters for Words

### Definition

> "The writing by these is properly stiled Stenography, or short-hand; Scripturae compendium, cum verba non perscribimus, sed signamus, saith Lypsius."

The art is "to contrive such figures for several syllables, as may easily be joined together in one form, according as different words shall require."

### The Angular Monogram for Jesus

> "Thus it is ordinary to represent any proper name by some such unusual character, as may contain in it all the letters of that name for which it is intended."

The Greek monogram **ΙΧΘΥΣ** (ichthys — fish), which resolves into **Ιησους Χριστος Θεου Υιος Σωτηρ** (Jesus Christ, Son of God, Saviour).

Wilkins describes a specific angular figure used by the Greeks that resolved into the letters **υγιεια** (health).

### Antiochus Soter's Symbol

> "This mark was esteemed so sacred amongst the ancients, that Antiochus Soter, a perpetual conqueror, did always instamp it upon his coin, and inscribe it on his ensigns; unto which he did pretend to be admonished in a dream, by an apparition of Alexander the Great."

In a fascinating contemporary detail, Wilkins notes:

> "There are many superstitious women in these times, who believe this to be so lucky a character, that they always work it upon the swaddling clothes of their young children, thinking thereby to make them more healthful and prosperous in their lives."

### Magic Characters

Some refer "the characters that are used in magic" to this category — "maintained to have, not only a secret signification, but likewise a natural efficacy."

### Shorthand in Wilkins's Time

> "This short-hand writing is now so ordinary in practice (it being usual for any common mechanic both to write and invent it) that I shall not need to set down any particular example of it."

By 1641, shorthand was already widespread. But anciently it was rarer and divided into:

- **Private** — personal codes for distinguished Romans to distinguish sincere letters from form letters. Hence the proverb *de meliori nota commendare* (to commend with the better mark)
- **Public** — standardized characters like those explained by Valerius Probus and collected by Janus Gruterus

### Invention: Tyro, Cicero's Servant

> "The first invention of them is commonly ascribed to Tyro, who was a servant unto Cicero."

But Wilkins notes competing claims:
- Trithemius said Cicero himself wrote a treatise, later augmented by St. Cyprian
- Trithemius found "in an old library, the copy of a psalter written in these characters, inscribed by some ignorant man with this title, *Psalterium in Lingua Armenica*"
- Cicero's letter to Atticus (13.32) mentions writing *δια σημειων* (in notes)
- Pet. Diaconus attributes the first invention to the poet Ennius, with additions by Tyro, Philargirus, Aquila, and Seneca the father — reaching 5,000 characters

### Biblical Claims

Hermannus Hugo, a Jesuit, claimed biblical precedent:
- Psalm 45:1 — "the pen of a ready writer" — alludes to shorthand
- Daniel 5:25 — the writing on the wall was in shorthand characters, which is why the Chaldean wizards couldn't read it

Wilkins is skeptical but allows that "the use of these word-characters may well enough conduce to the secrecy of any written message."

## Hieroglyphics

### Etymology and Origin

> "The word signifies sacred sculptures, which were engraven upon pillars, obelisks, pyramids, and other monuments, before the invention of letters."

The Egyptians expressed their minds "by the pictures of such creatures as did bear in them some natural resemblance to the thing intended."

### Examples

- **Bee** = king (industry, honey, and a sting)
- **Serpent with tail in mouth** = the year (returns into itself)
- **Cross** = *spem venturae salutis* or *vitam eternam* (hope of future salvation, eternal life) — "a kind of prophetical hieroglyphic"

Philó Judaeus listed hieroglyphic knowledge among Moses's Egyptian arts, and Clement of Alexandria related that Pythagoras submitted to circumcision to be admitted to these mysteries.

### The Scythian Message to Darius

> "A bird, a mouse, a frog, and certain arrows; intimating, that unless the Persians could fly as birds, or hide themselves under water as frogs, or inhabit the caverns of the earth as mice, they should not escape the Scythian arrows."

### Roman Military Signs

When silence and secrecy were required, the Romans lifted up a **minotaur** — "thereby teaching the captains, that their counsels and contrivances must be as inextricable as a labyrinth."

### The Egyptian Temple of Minerva

Plutarch describes a temple with images of an infant, an old man, a hawk (God), a fish (hatred), and a sea-horse (impudence):
> *"O ye that are born to die, know that God hateth impudence."*

## Emblems

### Definition

Emblems were "inserted as ornaments, upon vessels of gold, and other matters of state or pleasure" — the stamps of ancient medals, impresses of arms, frontispieces of books.

### Two Kinds

1. **Natural**: Grounded in resemblance to the thing itself
   - Dolphin on anchor = *festina lente* (deliberation in counsel, dispatch in execution)
   - Young stork carrying old one = filial gratitude

2. **Historical**: Reference to common stories
   - Prometheus gnawed by vulture = desert of over-much curiosity
   - Phaeton = folly of rashness
   - Narcissus = punishment of self-love

> "It was formerly esteemed a great sign of wit and invention, handsomely to convey any noted saying under such kind of expressions."

## Key Quotes
