# Chapter 19: Fabulous and Magical Methods

Wilkins divides long-distance visual communication into three categories: **fabulous**, **magical**, and **natural and true**. This chapter covers the first two — the mistaken and the suspect — clearing the ground before Chapter 20's practical systems.

## 1. Fabulous: The Sympathetic Magnet Needles

The most celebrated "secret" of Wilkins's age was the **magnetic telegraph** — two compass needles, both touched by the same loadstone, that would move in perfect sympathy at any distance.

> "Let there be two needles provided, of an equal length and bigness, being both of them touched with the same loadstone: let the letters of the alphabet be placed in the circles on which they are moved, as the points of the compass under the needle of the mariner's chart."

Two friends agree on times to communicate. One moves his needle to point at a letter; the other needle, "by a sympathy," moves to the same letter "though they be never so far distant." By repeated movements, they spell out messages.

The idea was popularised by Famianus Strada in his imitation of Lucretius's style:

> *O utinam haec ratio scribendi prodeat usu;*
> *Cautior et citior properaret epistola, nullas*
> *Latronum verita insidias, fluviosque morantes...*

("If only this method of writing were practical — letters would travel more safely and swiftly, fearing no ambushes of thieves, no delaying rivers...")

**Wilkins's refutation:**
1. "Every natural agent is supposed to have some certain sphere, which determines its activity" — magnetism has limited range.
2. Magnetic operations don't arise from "a sympathetical conformation of natures, which is the same at all distances" but from "a diffusion of magnetical qualities through the medium" — which falls off with distance.
3. The actual maximum range of magnetic communication is "two or three feet, or thereabouts" — quoting S. Ward's *Magnetis Reductio*.

He dryly cites Cabaeus: *Non solum exhibilandi sunt, sed etiam male mulctandi philosophica ferula, fabularum isti procursores* — "They should not only be laughed at, but beaten with the philosophical rod, those peddlers of fables."

## The Origin of the Magnet Myth

The loaded needle fantasy arose from two observed facts about the loadstone:
- It works through solid bodies (tables, walls)
- It has a directive virtue toward the poles

From these, people wrongly inferred it could operate at any distance.

## 2. Magical: Writing in the Moon

### Pythagoras's Moon Writing

The story: Pythagoras could "write any thing in the body of the Moon, so as it might be legible to another at a great distance." Agrippa claimed this was "naturally possible" — writing with blood upon a great glass, then opposing it against the full moon, so the letters would appear in the moon's circumference.

> *Quae cogitasset sanguine perscripsit in speculo, quo, ad pleni luminis lunae orbem obverso, stanti a tergo, res exaratas in disco lunae commonstravit.*

Wilkins notes the key detail: the writing would only be visible to someone standing between the glass and the moon — not at a great distance. The "wonder of the relation ceases."

### Enchanted Glasses

Some magicians claimed to possess glasses containing familiar spirits that could show "what was done in any part of the World, what ships were sailing in the Mediterranean, who were walking in any street of any City in Spain."

Wilkins relates a personal encounter: "I have heard a great pretender to the knowledge of all secret Arts, confidently affirm, that he himself was able at that time, or any other, to shew me in a glass what was done in any part of the World." The man was "an Italian Doctor of Physick" known for "extraordinary skill in the abstruser Arts, but not altogether free from the suspicion of this unlawful Magick."

## Optics: The Real Basis

There is a real optical experiment: write letters in wax on a mirror in inverted form; the glass, reflecting sunlight onto a shaded wall, will display them right-way-round. Wilkins initially thought this was what Godwin's *Nuntius Inanimatus* referred to. But reflection is too weak for distances — "the glass and the letters must be very big, and the wall somewhat near" — certainly not to the moon.

Wilkins dismisses the entire category: "Nor is there any other natural means conceivable, by which so strange an effect may be performed, which is the reason that it is so frequently attributed to diabolical Magic, by almost all the Writers that have occasion to treat of it."
