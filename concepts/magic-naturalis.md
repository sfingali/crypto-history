# Magia Naturalis

*Magia Naturalis* (Natural Magic, 1558) is the most famous work of [Giambattista della Porta](entities/porta-baptista.md), a compendium of natural philosophy, optical experiments, chemical recipes, and cryptographic methods. Wilkins cites it extensively in *Mercury*, particularly for:

- **Secret ink recipes** ([Chapter 5](concepts/ch-5-materials.md)) — including sal ammoniac, onion juice, lemon juice, and alum solutions
- **The cipher disk** — Porta's mechanical substitution device
- **The siege of Navarre story** — defenders signalling for supplies via cannon-fire patterns

The book's title reflects a key 16th/17th-century distinction: "natural magic" meant the investigation of nature's hidden properties (magnetism, optics, chemistry) — not supernatural conjuration but prototypical experimental science. Porta also founded the *Accademia dei Secreti* (Academy of Secrets) in Naples, one of the earliest scientific societies, which required members to present new discoveries in natural philosophy — a precursor to the [Royal Society](entities/royal-society.md) that Wilkins would help found.

For Wilkins, Porta represented the best of Renaissance cryptology: a systematiser who collected and tested methods rather than merely repeating classical authorities. But Wilkins also notes Porta's confession that some ciphers are genuinely unbreakable — *furore simile esse videtur, sibi aliquem persuadere tam circumspectum hominem esse posse, ut se a furtivo quodam scripto abditaque machinatione tueri possit* ("it seems like madness to persuade oneself that one can be so circumspect as to guard against some secret writing and hidden contrivance").
