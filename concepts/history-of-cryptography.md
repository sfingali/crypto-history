# History of Cryptography

A timeline of major developments in secret communication, as traced by John Wilkins in *Mercury* and extended by later scholarship.

## Classical Antiquity

- **Polybius** (2nd century BCE): 5×5 grid coordinate cipher; 10-torch signalling system → [[polybius-cipher]]
- **Caesar** (1st century BCE): D-for-A shift cipher → [[ch-7-equal-letters-powers]]
- **Aeneas Tacticus** (4th century BCE): earliest known military cipher treatise; scytale, knotted strings → [[ch-4-secret-conveyances]], [[ch-5-materials]]

## Hebrew Cabalism

- **ATBASH**, **Albam**, and **Notarikon** — methods embedded in the Hebrew scriptures, cited by Wilkins as evidence of cryptography's ancient roots → [[atbash]], [[notarikon]]

## Medieval

- **Bede** (8th century): finger-signing system for numbers → [[bede]], [[ch-14-signs-gestures]]
- **Roger Bacon** (13th century): secret writing methods → [[bacon-francis]]
- **Albertus Magnus** (13th century): the speaking brazen head legend → [[ch-17-joining-secrecy-swiftness-sound]]

## Renaissance

- **Trithemius** (1499–1518): *Steganographia* and *Polygraphia* — first printed cryptography texts → [[trithemius]]
- **Cardano** (1550): grille cipher → [[cardan-grille]]
- **Porta** (1563): cipher disk, *De Furtivis Literarum Notis* → [[porta-baptista]], [[porta-cipher-disk]]
- **Bacon** (1623): biliteral cipher (first binary alphabet) → [[bacon-cipher]]

## 17th Century — The English School

- **Godwin** (1629/1638): *Nuntius Inanimatus* and *The Man in the Moone* — the fiction that inspired Wilkins → [[francis-godwin]]
- **Wilkins** (1641): *Mercury* — first English cryptology text; comprehensive survey of all known methods → [[mercury-wilkins]]
- **Wilkins** (1668): *Essay towards a Real Character* — universal language project → [[universal-language-project]]
- **Royal Society** (1660): institutionalisation of experimental science → [[royal-society]]

## Post-Wilkins

- **Leibniz** (late 17th century): *characteristica universalis*, influenced by Wilkins → [[leibniz]]
- **Chappe** (1792): optical telegraph — mechanical realisation of Wilkins's torch relay concept
- **Morse** (1837): electrical telegraph — the binary alphabet becomes the foundation of modern communication
