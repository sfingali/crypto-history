# Porta's Cipher Disk

A mechanical substitution cipher device invented by [Giambattista della Porta](entities/porta-baptista.md) and described in his *De Furtivis Literarum Notis* (1563). The disk consists of two concentric alphabet rings; the outer ring is fixed, the inner ring rotates. By setting the inner ring to a pre-agreed position, each outer letter maps to a different inner letter — a physical key-character system.

Wilkins references Porta's work and cipher methods throughout *Mercury*, particularly in [Chapter 7](concepts/ch-7-equal-letters-powers.md) (substitution ciphers). The disk is the mechanical realisation of the key-character principle — where a keyword determines which of several alphabets is used for each letter, an ancestor of the Vigenère cipher.

Porta's contribution goes beyond the device itself: he was among the first to treat cryptography as a **systematic science** rather than a collection of ad hoc tricks. Wilkins, who shared this systematic ambition, treats Porta as a predecessor and authority throughout *Mercury*.
