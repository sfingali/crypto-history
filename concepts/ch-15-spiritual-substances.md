# Chapter 15: Swiftness by Qualities and Spiritual Substances

Wilkins transitions from secrecy (Part 1) to **swiftness** (Part 2). This chapter considers the fastest possible means of communication: thought itself, light, and angels.

## The Speed of Thought and Imagination

> "There is nothing (we say) so swift as thought, and yet the impression of these in another, might be as quick almost as the first act, if there were but such a great power in imagination, as some later philosophers have attributed to it."

Wilkins references Marsilio Ficino, Pomponazzi, and Paracelsus — Renaissance philosophers who attributed quasi-magical powers to the imagination — but seems skeptical.

## Light as a Swift Medium

> "Next to the acts of thought, the species of sight do seem to be of the quickest motion. We see the light of the east will in a moment fill the hemisphere, and the eye does presently discern an object that is very remote."

This connects to the optical telegraph methods in Chapter 20. Wilkins promises to "discourse afterwards" about how visual signals can communicate at great distances.

## Angels: The Swiftest Created Substances

> "Amongst all created substances, there are not any of so swift a motion as angels or spirits. Because there is not either within their natures, any such indisposition and relucctancy, or without them in the medium, any such impediment as may in the least manner retard their courses."

### Angels as Causers of Celestial Motion

> "Therefore have the ancient philosophers employed these as the causes of that mad celerity of the celestial orbs."

Wilkins offers a characteristically whimsical comparison: "I think it would be a hard match, if there were a race to be run betwixt the *primum mobile* and an angel. It being granted that neither of them could move in an instant, it would be but an even lay which should prove the swifter."

### The Etymology of "Angel"

> "From the fitness of spirits in this regard to convey any message, are they in the learned languages called messengers." — **מלאך** (Hebrew), **αγγελος** (Greek), **angelus** (Latin).

## Historical Apparitions as Messengers

### Socrates's Tutelary Genius

> "Now if a man had but such familiarity with one of these, as Socrates is said to have with his tutelary genius; if we could send but one of them upon any errand, there would be no quicker way than this for the dispatch of business at all distances."

### Vatinius's Vision

> "Vatinius being at Rome, was informed by an apparition of that victory which Paulus their general had obtained over king Perses in Macedon, the very same day wherein the battle was fought; which was a long time before any other messenger could arrive with the news."

### Sympathy or Guardian Angel?

Wilkins discusses stories of people knowing a friend's death at the moment it happened — "either by bleeding, or by dreams, or some such way of intimation." While commonly attributed to "sympathy," Wilkins proposes an alternative:

> "It is more probably to be ascribed unto the spirit or genius. There being a more especial acquaintance and commerce betwixt the tutelary angels of particular friends, they are sometimes by them informed (though at great distances) of such remarkable accidents as befall one another."

## The Unsatisfactory Conclusion

> "But this way there is little hopes to advantage our enquiry, because it is not so easy to employ a good angel, nor safe dealing with a bad one."

Wilkins dismisses angelic messengers as impractical — we can neither command good angels nor safely traffic with demons.

## Trithemius and the Saturnine Angels

### The Burned Manuscript

Trithemius, in his books on secret communication, "does pretend to handle the forms of conjuration, calling each kind of character by the name of spirits, thereby to deter the vulgar from searching into his works."

But some believe he went beyond metaphor:

> "Especially in one place, where he speaks of the three saturnine angels, and certain images, by which, in the space of twenty-four hours, a man may be informed of news from any part of the world."

This passage led Frederick II, Prince Palatine (on the advice of Junius), to **burn the original manuscript** of Trithemius's work. Wilkins notes that Selenus blamed this action as unjust.

In Chapter 20, Wilkins will suggest an alternative reading: Trithemius may have been describing signal-station networks (like beacon chains), not actual angels — "that author might then in one respect, be freed from the aspersion of diabolical magic."
