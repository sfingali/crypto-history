# Chapter 4: Secret Conveyances of Written Messages

This chapter catalogs the **physical concealment** of written messages — not cryptography (hiding meaning) but steganography (hiding the message's existence). Wilkins draws on a vast range of classical sources to collect every trick in the ancient playbook.

## The Framework

> "The secrecy of any written message may consist either in the *Conveyance*, or the *Writing*."

Chapters 4 deals with conveyance — hiding the letter in its carriage. Later chapters deal with the writing itself.

Three categories:
1. **By Land** — through guarded checkpoints and sealed borders
2. **By Water** — across rivers, under naval blockades
3. **Through the Open Air** — arrows, slings, birds

## By Land: Ancient Tricks

### Harpagus and the Hare's Belly (Herodotus)

> "Harpagus the Mede... puts his letters into the belly of a hare, which, together with certain hunters' nets, he delivered unto a trusty servant, who under this disguise of a huntsman, got an unsuspected passage to Cyrus."

This was the message that triggered Cyrus's revolt against his grandfather Astyages — "bereaving him of that kingdom which was then the greatest monarchy in the world." The stakes of secret communication could hardly be higher.

### Demaratus and the Wax Tablet (Justin/Herodotus)

Demaratus, the exiled Spartan king at Xerxes's Persian court, learned of the planned invasion of Greece. He "wrote an epistle in a tablet of wood, covered over the letters with wax," and sent it to Sparta. The magistrates were "for a long time in a perplexed consultation what it should mean" until "the king's sister did accidentally discover the writing under the wax." The forewarned Greeks defeated "the greatest and most numerous army that is mentioned in history."

### Council of Ephesus — The Beggar Disguise

When Nestorius was condemned, the Council fathers, "being strictly debarred from all ordinary ways of conveyances, were fain to send unto Constantinople by one in the disguise of a beggar."

### Stranger Concealments

- **Coffins**: "Some messengers have been sent away in coffins as being dead"
- **Animal disguise**: Josephus recounts messengers who "crept out of the city by night like dogs" at the siege of Jotapata
- **Food concealment**: Polycrita sent letters in food to imprisoned friends; Laurentius Medicus hid epistles in bread
- **Wax candle**: A letter rolled inside a candle, with instructions that "the candle would give him light for his business"
- **Leaves on wounds** (Aeneas): Writing on leaves and covering a "putrid ulcer" with them — where the enemy would never search
- **Flesh**: Letters inscribed on the messenger's own body (Ovid: *"inque suo corpore verba ferat"*)

### Hystiaeus: The Most Extreme Example

> "But amongst all the ancient practices in this kind, there is none for the strangeness, to be compared unto that of Hystiaeus."

Hystiaeus, residing at Darius's Persian court, needed to send a message to Aristagoras in Greece about revolt. His method:

1. Shaved the head of a trusted servant with "sore eyes"
2. Imprinted the secret message on the servant's scalp
3. Kept him confined until the hair grew back
4. Told him to travel to Aristagoras, who would "by shaving his hair the second time, would certainly restore him"

> "By which relation you may see what strange shifts the ancients were put unto, for want of skill in this subject that is here discoursed of."

### Bonus: Swallowed Gold

Some fugitive Jews at the siege of Jerusalem melted gold into bullets and swallowed them, recovering them later among "other excrements." Wilkins muses: if a man could write Homer's Iliads in a nut-shell (as some boasted), he could use this trick for "a whole pacquet of letters."

## By Water

When land passages were blocked:

- **Lead plates**: Writing on thin lead and fastening them to a swimmer's arms or thighs
- **Bladders**: Lucullus informed a besieged city of his coming by putting letters between two bladders, with a soldier "in the disguise of a sea-monster" swimming them in
- **Underwater passage**: More "exquisite inventions" to pass underwater, either personally or in a boat, with "a long trunk or pipe, with a tunnel at the top of it, to let down fresh air"

Countermeasures: the ancients would "cross the rivers with strong nets" and "fasten stakes in several parts of the channel with sharp irons, as the blades of swords."

## Through the Open Air

### Arrows

David and Jonathan's agreed signal (1 Samuel 20), which Wilkins notes "does somewhat savour of the ancient simplicity and rudeness." More sophisticated: Artabazus and Timoxenus fastened letters to arrows directed to appointed places.

Cleonymus, king of Lacedaemon, besieging Trezene, shot arrows into the town with notes: *"I come that I may restore this place to its liberty"* — upon which "the credulous and discontented inhabitants were very willing to let him enter."

Caesar encouraged Cicero's besieged forces by shooting an arrow with: *"Caesar Ciceroni: fiduciam opta, expecta auxilia."* The soldiers held out until the succour arrived.

### Slings and Lead Bullets

Appian records that the besieged wrote messages on small pieces of lead and cast them with slings to exactly appointed places. Wilkins notes the German wars had recently invented "bullets" that could shoot "not only letters, corn, and the like, but (which is the strangest) powder also into a besieged city."

### Birds (Foreshadowing)

Briefly mentioned as "pigeons and swallows, instead of messengers," to be treated "more particularly in the sixteenth chapter."

### The Flying Chariot

> "Imagination itself cannot conceive any one more useful, than the invention of a flying chariot... Since by this means a man may have as free a passage as a bird, which is not hindered, either by the highest walls, or the deepest rivers and trenches, or the most watchful centinels."

Wilkins had discussed flying chariots in his first book, *The Discovery of a World in the Moone* (chapter 14), and would return to the subject in *Mathematical Magick* (1648).

## Key Quotes
