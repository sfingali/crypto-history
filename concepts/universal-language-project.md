# Wilkins's Universal Language Project

The **universal philosophical language** was [John Wilkins's](entities/john-wilkins.md) most ambitious intellectual project — a system of writing in which symbols represent **things and concepts directly**, bypassing spoken language entirely. First articulated in seed form in [Chapter 13](concepts/ch-13-universal-character.md) of [*Mercury*](entities/mercury-wilkins.md) (1641), the project consumed Wilkins for 27 years and culminated in *An Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language* (1668) — a folio of over 600 pages, published under the sponsorship of the [Royal Society](entities/royal-society.md) and dedicated to its president.

## The Vision

The central insight is deceptively simple:

> "If there were such an universal character to express things and notions, as might be legible to all people and countries, so that men of several nations might with the same ease both write and read it, this invention would be a far greater advantage in this particular, and mightily conduce to the spreading and promoting of all arts and sciences: because that great part of our time which is now required to the learning of words, might then be employed in the study of things."

The radicalism of this: every educated European spent years learning Latin (and often Greek and Hebrew) — years that could be spent learning **what is actually true about the world**. A universal character would redirect human intellectual effort from **grammar** to **knowledge**.

### Pronunciation Independence

The key design feature:

> "In the reading of such a writing, though men of several countries should each of them differ in their voices, and pronouncing several words, yet the sense would be still the same."

A Jew, a Greek, an Italian, a Frenchman, a German, and an Englishman all see the sign **♉** (Taurus). Each pronounces it differently — שור, Ταυρον, toro, taureau, Stier, "a bull" — but "though several nations may differ in the expression of things, yet they all agree in the same conceit of them."

This is the essence of the project: **written concepts, not written sounds**.

## Precedents and Proofs of Concept

In Chapter 13, Wilkins marshals several existing systems as evidence that universal writing is possible:

### 1. Numbers

The digits 1, 2, 3... and Roman numerals I, II, V, X... are understood across languages. So are the symbols for weights and measures (℈ for scruple, ʒ for drachm).

### 2. Astronomical Signs

The zodiac symbols (♈ Aries, ♉ Taurus, ♊ Gemini...) and planetary symbols (♄ Saturn, ♃ Jupiter, ♂ Mars...) are used internationally. Wilkins suggests these were invented by ancient astrologers "for the secrecy of them, the better to conceal their sacred and mysterious profession from vulgar capacity" — but whatever their origin, they demonstrate the principle.

### 3. Chemical Symbols

Alchemical and chemical notation — ☿ for mercury, ♀ for copper (associated with Venus), 🜹 for salt ammoniac — crosses language boundaries. A German alchemist and an Italian alchemist could read each other's symbols.

### 4. Musical Notes

The five-line staff and note shapes (♩, ♪, ♫) are a genuinely universal notation — a 17th-century musician in London could sight-read music written in Venice.

> "Nor is there any reason why there may not be such a general kind of writing invented for the expression of every thing else as well as these particulars."

### 5. Chinese Characters

Wilkins cites the Jesuit missionary Trigaultius's account of China and Japan: though their spoken languages differ "almost as much as Hebrew and Dutch," either people "can, by this help of a common character, as well understand the books and letters of the others, as if they were only their own."

Chinese writing — where characters represent meanings, not sounds — is the real-world proof that a universal character works.

## The 1668 *Essay*: How It Worked

After 27 years of development, Wilkins published his system. It was not a language in the conventional sense but a **taxonomy of all knowledge**, structured as a hierarchical classification:

### The Classification Scheme

1. **40 Genera** (major categories): e.g., *World*, *Element*, *Stone*, *Metal*, *Plant*, *Animal*, *Man*, *Family Relation*, *Judicial Relation*, *Military Relation*, etc.

2. Each genus divided into **Differences** (subcategories): e.g., under *Animal* → *Beast*, *Bird*, *Fish*, *Insect*, etc.

3. Each difference divided into **Species** (specific things): e.g., under *Beast* → *Dog*, *Cat*, *Horse*, etc.

### The Notation

Each concept was represented by a compound symbol:
- A **base character** indicating the genus
- **Modifying marks** (hooks, loops, strokes) indicating the difference and species
- **Additional marks** for grammatical functions (plural, tense, part of speech)

The system aimed for **seven or eight thousand** basic characters — comparable to Chinese — each systematically derived from the classification.

### The Tables

The *Essay* included fold-out tables mapping the entire classification — the first modern attempt at a complete ontology of human knowledge. These tables influenced:
- **Linnaeus's taxonomy** (the hierarchical classification of species)
- **Roget's Thesaurus** (the thematic classification of words)
- **Dewey Decimal Classification** (the systematic ordering of library subjects)

## The Seed in *Mercury* (Chapter 13)

The 1641 chapter is a manifesto, not a working system. Wilkins had the vision at 27 but not the execution. The chapter:

1. **States the problem**: The curse of Babel — language confusion as a barrier to knowledge
2. **Identifies precedents**: Existing universal notations (numbers, astronomy, chemistry, music, Chinese)
3. **Sketches the method**: Primitive words + derivative marks, following the Hebrew model of fewest radicals
4. **Estimates the scale**: Seven or eight thousand characters — "not more difficult than the learning of any one language"
5. **Issues the call**: "The perfecting of such an invention were the only way to unite the seventy-two languages of the first confusion; and therefore may very well deserve their endeavours who have both abilities and leisure for such kind of enquiries"

The chapter ends with this open invitation — and Wilkins spent the rest of his life answering it himself.

## Influence and Reception

### On Leibniz

[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](entities/leibniz.md) read the *Essay* soon after publication and corresponded about it extensively. He admired Wilkins's ambition but found the execution insufficiently philosophical: Wilkins's symbols were arbitrary rather than logically derived from first principles. Leibniz's *characteristica universalis* aimed higher — a language where truth could be **calculated** by manipulating symbols — but the starting point was the same: concepts before words, reason before grammar.

### On Dalgarno

The Scottish linguist **George Dalgarno** published *Ars Signorum* ("The Art of Signs," 1661) — a competing universal language — seven years before Wilkins's *Essay*. Dalgarno used a similar classification system but with a different notation. Wilkins acknowledged Dalgarno in his preface, and the two projects were compared by contemporaries. Dalgarno's system was more elegant but less comprehensive.

### On Constructed Languages

The 17th-century universal language movement (Wilkins, Dalgarno, Lodwick, Beck, Urquhart) was the **first wave of constructed language design**. It differed from later movements (Esperanto, Volapük) in aiming not at a new spoken language but at a **written philosophical system** — classification first, communication second.

### Royal Society Sponsorship

The *Essay* was published under the Society's imprimatur — a mark of institutional endorsement. Wilkins was Bishop of Chester and a founding Fellow. The Society's involvement demonstrates that 17th-century science saw linguistics and classification as part of its remit — not separate from natural philosophy but integral to it.

## Why It Failed (and What Survived)

The *Essay* failed as a practical universal language for several reasons:

1. **Complexity**: Seven thousand characters was too many
2. **Rigidity**: The classification embedded 17th-century knowledge, which quickly became obsolete
3. **Arbitrariness**: Why 40 genera? Why these differences? The scheme was under-motivated
4. **No speakers**: No community ever adopted it

But the project's **intellectual legacy** is immense:

- **Classification systems**: Linnaeus, Roget, Dewey, and modern ontologies all descend from Wilkins's tables
- **Semantics**: The idea that meaning is structured — that concepts form a taxonomy — anticipates modern semantic theory
- **Logic**: Leibniz's more rigorous approach fed into Frege and modern mathematical logic
- **Knowledge representation**: Modern AI and the Semantic Web grapple with the same problem Wilkins faced: how to represent meaning independently of any particular language

Wilkins's dream — that "the confusion at Babel might this way have been remedied" — remains unrealised. But the project of building systematic bridges between languages, through translation, classification, and logic, traces a continuous line from the Bishop of Chester's folio to the algorithms that translate this page.
