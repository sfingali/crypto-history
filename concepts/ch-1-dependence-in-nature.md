# Chapter 1: The Dependence of This Knowledge in Nature

Wilkins opens *Mercury* by establishing **why secret and swift communication matters** — grounding it in human nature, divine design, and the history of learning.

## The Argument

### Communication as a Natural Necessity

> "Every rational creature, being of an imperfect and dependent happiness, is therefore naturally endowed with an ability to communicate its own thoughts and intentions; that so by mutual services, it might the better promote itself in the prosecution of its own well-being."

Wilkins begins with a theological anthropology: because we are imperfect and dependent, nature has given us the faculty of communication so we can help each other. This is the book's foundation — secret and swift messaging isn't a curiosity but a fulfilment of our natural design.

### Three Modes of Created Communication

Wilkins distinguishes two kinds of beings and their modes of discourse:

1. **Angels (spiritual substances)**: communicate *per insinuationem specierum* — by directly "unveiling their own natures" to each other's knowledge. Since angels are immaterial, they "hear, and know, and speak, not with several parts, but with their whole substance." He cites Aquinas and Zanchius.

2. **Humans (organical bodies)**: cannot communicate so directly. We need "corporeal instruments" — the **ear** as "the sense of discipline or learning" and the **tongue** as "the instrument of teaching." The communion between these is **speech or language**.

### Language is Not Innate

> "Languages are so far natural unto us, as other arts and sciences. A man is born without any of them, but yet capable of all."

Wilkins argues against the idea that any language is natural. He critiques experiments where people tried to discover the "primitive tongue" by raising infants in silence to see what they would speak — calling the reasoning behind such enquiries "absurd." A child raised to speak another tongue would not retain knowledge of a "natural" language, and "most men should be of a double language, which is evidently false."

### Writing: Communication Across Time and Space

Words serve only those present in time and place, so the invention of **letters and writing** was added. Wilkins quotes Pliny on the divinity of writing and tells a vivid anecdote:

> **The Indian Slave and the Figs**: An Indian slave, sent by his master with a basket of figs and a letter, eats a great part of them on the way. The recipient reads the letter and accuses him. The slave, "cursing the paper as being a false and lying witness," denies everything. Sent again with more figs and another letter, he first hides the letter under a stone — "assuring himself, that if it did not see him eat the figs, it could never tell of him." When accused again, he "confesses the fault, admiring the divinity of the paper."

This story, which Wilkins takes from Hermannus Hugo, illustrates the sheer strangeness of writing to those who first encounter it — and frames the discussion of newer, stranger forms of communication.

### Egyptian Mercury: The First Inventor

Writing was first invented by "the Egyptian Mercury," who is therefore "stiled the messenger of the Gods." The poets gave him wings for swiftness, and because the planet Mercury has "a more various and obscure revolution than any of the rest," they attributed "secret and subtle motions" to him — making him "a trusty and private messenger." This is the mythological justification for the book's title.

### Signs and Gestures

Wilkins notes a third mode: **signs and gestures**, which are prior to speech in nature since "infants are able this way to express themselves, before they have the benefit of speech." This foreshadows Chapter 14's treatment of *semaeologia*.

### Why Secrecy Matters

> "The ignorance of secret and swift conveyances hath often proved fatal, not only to the ruin of particular persons, but also of whole armies and kingdoms."

The stakes are high — hence the enterprise is "worth our enquiry."

## Survey of Authors

Wilkins positions his book within a learned tradition:

**Ancients**: Aeneas, Cleomenes, Democritus (cited by Polybius), Julius Africanus, Philo Mechanicus (Greek). For military signals: Vegetius, Frontinus (Roman). For abbreviations: Valerius Probus, Pet. Diaconus, Janus Gruterus (ascribed to Cicero and Seneca).

**Moderns**: Abbot Trithemius, Theodorus Bibliander, Baptista Porta, Cardan, Isaac Casaubon, Johannes Walchius, Gustavus Selenus, Gerardus Vossius, Hermannus Hugo, and "divers others."

**Bacon**: Wilkins gives special honour to "our English Aristotle, the learned Verulam" (Francis Bacon), who in *The Advancement of Learning* "hath briefly contracted the whole substance of what may be said in this subject" and referred it to the "deficient part" of grammar.

### Relation to Grammar

The subject belongs to **grammar** in its "true latitude" — which comprehends "all the ways of discourse, whether by speech, or by writing, or by gesture." Wilkins deploys a financial metaphor:

> "Expressions being current for conceits, as money is for valuations."

Just as a merchant needs to understand different kinds of money, so those who profess knowledge need to understand different modes of expression.

## The Book's Three-Part Structure

Wilkins announces his programme:

1. **Secrecy** — means to communicate thoughts privately
2. **Swiftness** — quick passing at great distances
3. **Joining both** — secrecy and swiftness combined

He will also mention "magical or fabulous" methods "received upon common tradition" alongside the true ones.
