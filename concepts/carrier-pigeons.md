# Carrier Pigeons: A History of Pigeon Post

The use of **carrier pigeons** (homing pigeons) for message delivery is one of the oldest and most reliable forms of long-distance communication. [John Wilkins](entities/john-wilkins.md), in [Chapter 16](concepts/ch-16-swiftness-by-bodies.md) of [*Mercury*](entities/mercury-wilkins.md), provides a comprehensive anthology of pigeon-post stories from antiquity to his own time, arguing that "amongst all animate bodies, there is not any that have naturally so swift a motion as birds."

## The Pigeon's Talent

Homing pigeons possess an extraordinary ability: when transported away from their loft, they can **find their way home** over hundreds of miles, flying at speeds of 50–90 km/h. This talent — whose mechanisms (magnetic sensing, visual landmarks, olfactory cues) are still not fully understood — made them ideal one-way messengers. A pigeon carried from its home loft to a distant point will reliably fly back; by keeping pigeons at strategic locations, two-way communication was possible.

## Ancient Examples (from Wilkins)

Wilkins, drawing on classical sources, catalogues the earliest recorded uses of pigeon post:

### Hirtius and Brutus at Mutina (43 BCE)

The most famous classical pigeon-post episode. During the siege of **Mutina** (modern Modena) in the Roman civil wars, **Decimus Brutus** was besieged by Mark Antony. The consul **Aulus Hirtius** maintained communication with Brutus using pigeons trained to fly between the consular camp and the besieged city:

> "Tying their letters unto such pigeons, as were taught beforehand to fly from the tents to the city, and from thence to the tents again."

This allowed coordinated military action despite the besieging army. The pigeon post at Mutina was a decisive factor — Brutus could coordinate sorties with relief forces.

### Taurosthenes's Olympic Victory (Aelian)

Taurosthenes, a citizen of Aegina, won a victory at the **Olympic Games**. To inform his father on the island of Aegina immediately, he released a pigeon that he had brought from home — a pigeon dyed purple (the colour of victory). The bird flew back to Aegina, arriving before any human messenger could make the journey by sea or land:

> "Thaurosthenes, in the same day that he had got the victory at the Olympic games, did by a pigeon sent from thence, bring news of it to his father in Aegina."

This is one of the earliest recorded uses of pigeon post for civilian news — and the speed advantage (bird vs. boat) is the point.

### Roman Magistrates' Pigeons (Varro, via Lipsius)

Wilkins relays a more domestic use, sourced from Varro (the Roman scholar) through Justus Lipsius:

> "It was usual for the Roman magistrates when they went unto the theatre, or other such public meetings, whence they could not return at pleasure, to carry such a pigeon with them; that if any unexpected business should happen, they might thereby give warning to their friends or families at home."

This is pigeon post as **personal pager** — the magistrate attends the theatre but can be recalled by note tied to a pigeon released from home.

### Anacreon's Pet Pigeon

The Greek lyric poet **Anacreon** (6th century BCE) wrote an ode to a pet pigeon he used as a messenger — the earliest literary treatment of pigeon post. Wilkins mentions it in passing.

### Volaterranus's Victory Swallows

A variant: swallows, not pigeons. **Volaterranus** sent swallows from a battlefield back to Rome "anointed over with the colour of victory" to announce a military success. The colour-coding is a nice detail: the dyed bird *is* the message, no writing needed.

### Besieger's Knot-Tied String

A more complex variant: during a siege, the besieger captured a mother swallow whose young were in the city. He tied a knotted string to her leg — the number of knots indicating "after what number of days their aids would come" — and released her. The swallow flew to her nest, and the knots were read as a message. This combines zoological knowledge with pre-numerical signalling.

## Juvenal and the Literary Tradition

Wilkins cites Juvenal's satirical reference to the speed of pigeon post:

> *"Tanquam e diversis partibus orbis / Anxia praecipiti venisset epistola penna"*
> ("As if from distant parts of the world / An anxious letter had come on swift wing")

The satirist's image confirms that pigeon post was a familiar concept to Roman readers — fast, if not always reliable.

## Carrier Pigeons in Wilkins's Own Time

Wilkins emphasises that pigeon post was not merely ancient history but **current practice**:

> "Nor hath it been less used in these later times, especially in those countries where by reason of continual wars and dissensions, there have been more particular and urgent necessity for such kind of conveyances."

He quotes contemporary authorities:

- **Casaubon**: *"Nunc vulgatissima res est, columbas habere ad ejusmodi jussa paratas"* ("Now it is most common to keep pigeons ready for such commands")
- **Stewechius**: *"Harum opera nostrates hoc bello civili, frequenter adjuti sunt"* ("Our countrymen have frequently been aided by their service in this civil war")

The "civil war" referred to is likely the Dutch Revolt or the Thirty Years' War — the very conflicts that made secret communication urgent in Wilkins's Europe.

## The Name: "Carrier"

Wilkins ends his treatment with an etymology:

> "There is a smaller sort of pigeon, of a light body, and swift flight. Which is usually made choice of for such particulars; and therefore the kind of them is commonly called by the name of **Carriers**."

The English word "carrier pigeon" thus has a direct etymological link to the bird's function as a message-carrier — the name *is* the job description.

## In Wilkins's Larger Argument

Carrier pigeons serve a specific argumentative function in *Mercury*: they are **the swiftest natural messengers**, bridging the gap between the theoretical (angels and spirits, Chapter 15) and the merely fast (post-riders, foot-messengers). Birds are not supernatural, but they approach the speed of the supernatural through natural means.

More broadly, they are evidence for Wilkins's central claim: that **swiftness and secrecy in communication** are achievable through rational, natural methods — no magic required. Pigeons are a proof of concept: if a bird can do this, what might human ingenuity achieve with systematic thought?

## Later History

The carrier pigeon's career continued long after Wilkins:

- **1815**: Nathan Rothschild allegedly received news of Waterloo via pigeon post before the general public, enabling a lucrative market move (though the story may be exaggerated)
- **1870–71**: During the Siege of Paris in the Franco-Prussian War, pigeons carried microfilmed messages out of the city — over 150,000 official and 1 million private messages
- **WWI and WWII**: Military pigeon services on all sides; 32 pigeons awarded the Dickin Medal (animal VC) for gallantry
- **Modern sport**: Pigeon racing continues as a global sport, with birds routinely flying 500–1,000 km home

The "carrier" breed that Wilkins described was only the beginning.
