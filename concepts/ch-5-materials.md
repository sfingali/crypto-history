# Chapter 5: Secrecy in the Materials of Writing

Wilkins shifts from the physical concealment of messages (Chapter 4) to secrecy in the materials of writing themselves — the paper and the ink. This chapter inaugurates **Cryptographia** proper.

## Secrecy by the Paper: The Scytale

### The Lacedemonian Scytale

The chief ancient contrivance. Method:
1. Two round staves of equal length and size are prepared — one retained by the magistrates at home, one carried by the general abroad
2. To write a secret message, a "narrow thong of parchment" is wrapped around the staff in a "serpentine revolution" so that the edges meet
3. The epistle is inscribed along the edges
4. When the parchment is unwound, only "pieces of letters on the sides of it" appear — unintelligible without the correct scytale

Wilkins quotes Ausonius's description: *"Vel Lacedaemoniam scytalen imitare libelli, / Segmina pergameni tereti circumdata ligno..."*

### The Scytale's Weakness

Wilkins notes: "This way was not of such inextricable secrecy, but that a little examination might have easily discovered it (as Scaliger truly observes)." However:

> "It was too inconsiderate and magistral a sentence of him, from thence to conclude all this kind of learning to be vain and useless, serving only for impostures and to perplex the enquirer."

Wilkins argues with confidence that "there may be some ways of secrecy, which it were madness for any man to think he could unfold." He quotes Vegetius and Baptista Porta — even Porta, famous for his ability to discover secret writings, confessed that declaring oneself able to interpret any secret writing would be "madness and folly."

### The Scytale in Practice: Pharnabazus Deceives Lysander

Wilkins references Plutarch's account: Pharnabazus used a scytale to deceive Lysander.

## Secrecy by Ink: The Secret Inks

Wilkins catalogues an extensive list of chemical methods for invisible writing — a treasury of 17th-century "natural magic":

### Heat-Developed Inks

- **Salt armoniac** dissolved in water: letters appear only when held by fire
- **Juice of onions, lemons**: "and divers the like acid and corroding moistures"
- The ancients used these extensively

### Water-Developed Inks

- **Dissolved alum**: letters only discernible when the paper is dipped in water

### Light-Developed Inks

- Some juices "do not appear, till the paper be held betwixt a candle and the eye"

### Darkness-Developed Inks

- **Water of putrified willow** or **distilled juice of glow-worms**: visible only in the dark — Porta "affirms from his own experience"

### Dual-Ink Systems

Two inks of identical colour, but one easily rubbed or washed off — allowing a covert message to be erased while the overt one remains.

### The Egg Technique

> "A man may likewise write secretly with a raw egg, the letters of which being thoroughly dried, let the whole paper be blacked over with ink, that it may appear without any inscription; and when this ink is also well dried, if you do afterwards gently scrape it over with a knife, it will fall off from those places, where before the words were written."

### Glutinous Inks (Dust-Developed)

Milk, urine, fat, or other "glutinous moisture" — invisible until dust is scattered on the paper, adhering to the written parts. Wilkins quotes Ovid:

> *"Tuta quoque est, fallitque oculos e lacte recenti / Litera, carbonis pulvere tange, leges."*

### Attalus and the Entrail Trick

> "Attalus made use of this device, the better to excite the courage of his soldiers."

Before battle, Attalus sacrificed to the gods. He had previously written *Regis victoria* backwards in his hand with "some gummy juice." As the priest examined the entrails, the letters gathered enough dust to become legible — convincing the soldiers of divine favour. "By which omen the soldiers were so strangely heightened in their hopes and valour, that they won the day."

## The Knotted String and Thread-Through-Holes

### The Perforated Tablet

Draw a string through holes in a little board, where "these holes should be of the same number with the letters, unto which by compact they should be severally applied." The order of the string's passage through them "may serve to express any words, and so consequently any sense we would discover."

### The Knot Method

> "For who would mistrust any private news or treachery to lie hid in a thread, wherein there was nothing to be discerned, but sundry confused knots, or other the like marks?"

A square tablet with the 24 letters on top at equal distances, with "divers little teeth" on opposite sides. The string is hitched and fastened for "its several returns." Wilkins provides a detailed diagram showing how the string, when applied to the recipient's matching tablet, reveals the message. His example: the string encodes *"Beware of this bearer, who is sent as a spy over you."*

## Key Quotes

> "It is certain, that some occasions may require the exactest privacy; and it is as certain, that there may be some ways of secrecy, which it were madness for any man to think he could unfold."

> "Multa esse posse furtiva scripta, quae se interpretaturum quemquam polliceri, furorem et delirium plane existimarem." — Baptista Porta
