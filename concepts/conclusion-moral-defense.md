# Conclusion: The Moral Defense of Teaching Deception

The book's final pages address an objection Wilkins clearly anticipated: **is it responsible to teach people how to deceive?**

## Mercury the Trickster

Wilkins opens with classical precedent: the poets "feigned Mercury to be the chief Patron of Thieves and Treachery." Homer relates how Mercury "filched from Venus her Girdle," robbed Jupiter of his Scepter, and would have stolen his Thunderbolt too "but that he feared to burn his fingers." Astrologers observe that those born under the planet Mercury "are naturally addicted to Theft and Cheating."

The implication is clear: a book named *Mercury* teaching secret communication might be accused of enabling crime.

## The Counter-Argument: Knowledge Cuts Both Ways

Wilkins offers a layered defense:

### 1. Detection as Well as Deception

> "It does not only teach how to deceive, but consequently also how to discover Delusions."

Learning how ciphers work teaches you how to break them. Cryptography and cryptanalysis are two sides of the same coin.

### 2. Practical Limits

> "The chief experiments are of such nature, that they cannot be frequently practised, without just cause of suspicion, when as it is in the Magistrate's power to prevent them."

Most of the methods described (bell-towers, torch relays, smoke signals) are too conspicuous for casual criminal use.

### 3. The Writing Precedent

> "There is nothing hath more occasioned Troubles and Contention, than the Art of Writing, which is the reason why the Inventor of it is fabled to have sown Serpents Teeth."

Cadmus, legendary inventor of the alphabet, sowed dragon's teeth that sprang up as armed men. Writing itself caused wars. And yet:

> "It was but a barbarous act of Thamus, the Egyptian King, therefore to forbid the learning of Letters."

Thamus (in Plato's *Phaedrus*) rejected the god Thoth's gift of writing, fearing it would destroy memory. History judged him wrong.

### 4. The Tongue Argument

> "We may as well cut out our Tongues, because that member is a *world of wickedness* (James 3)."

If everything susceptible to abuse must be suppressed, nothing can be taught:

> "If all those useful Inventions that are liable to abuse, should therefore be concealed, there is not any Art or Science which might be lawfully professed."

## The Final Word

This is not just rhetoric. It's a philosophical position about the relationship between knowledge and ethics — one that would characterise the Royal Society (which Wilkins helped found) and the entire scientific revolution. Knowledge is neutral; its application is what matters. To suppress useful knowledge for fear of misuse is to abandon reason itself.

The conclusion transforms *Mercury* from a technical manual into a document of the early Enlightenment. Wilkins is not just cataloguing ciphers; he's arguing — in 1641, with civil war about to break out — that open knowledge is the right path even when that knowledge could be dangerous in the wrong hands.
