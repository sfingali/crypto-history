# Chapter 13: An Universal Character

This is the **seed chapter** of Wilkins's life's work. Here, at age 27, he first articulates the vision that would culminate 27 years later in his monumental *An Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language* (1668) — a system of writing that represents things directly, comprehensible to all nations regardless of their spoken language.

## The Two Curses of Man

> "After the fall of Adam, there were two general curses inflicted on mankind: the one upon their labours, the other upon their language."

Against labour, we strive through "all those common arts and professions about which the world is busied." Against the confusion of tongues, the best remedy so far is "the Latin tongue, and the other learned languages, which by reason of their generality, do somewhat restore us from the first confusion."

## The Vision

> "But now if there were such an universal character to express things and notions, as might be legible to all people and countries, so that men of several nations might with the same ease both write and read it, this invention would be a far greater advantage in this particular, and mightily conduce to the spreading and promoting of all arts and sciences: because that great part of our time which is now required to the learning of words, might then be employed in the study of things."

The key insight: learning languages consumes years that could be spent learning **things**. A universal character would redirect human intellectual effort from grammar to knowledge.

> "Nay, the confusion at Babel might this way have been remedied, if every one could have expressed his own meaning by the same kind of character. But then perhaps the art of letters was not invented."

## Precedents: The Chinese Model

Wilkins cites Trigaultius's account of China and Japan: though their spoken languages differ as much as Hebrew and Dutch, "either of them can, by this help of a common character, as well understand the books and letters of the others, as if they were only their own."

The Chinese writing system — where characters represent meanings, not sounds — is the real-world proof of concept.

## Existing Universal Characters in the West

Wilkins identifies several domains where universal signs are already in use:

### 1. Numbers

> "Many nations do agree in the characters of the common numbers, describing them either the Roman way by letters, as I. II. V. X. C. D. M. or else the Barbarian way by figures, as 1. 2. 3. 10. &c."

Weights, lengths, and capacities are expressed by characters understood across languages: ℈ (scruple), ʒ (drachm), and so on.

### 2. Astronomical Signs

> "The astronomers of several countries do express both the heavenly signs, and planets, and aspects by the same kind of notes."

♈, ♉, ♊, ♋, etc. for zodiac signs; ♄, ♃, ♂, ♀ for planets; □, △, ⚹, ☌ for aspects.

Wilkins suggests these were invented by ancient astrologers "for the secrecy of them, the better to conceal their sacred and mysterious profession from vulgar capacity."

### 3. Chemical Symbols

> "The chymical treatises that are written in different languages, do all of them agree in the same form of writing their minerals."

Planetary metals use their planet symbols; other substances have their own: 🜹 for salt ammoniac, 🜺 for arsenic.

### 4. Musical Notes

> "Musical notes in most countries are the same."

The five-line staff and note shapes transcend language.

> "Nor is there any reason why there may not be such a general kind of writing invented for the expression of every thing else as well as these particulars."

## How to Build It

### Primitive Words

> "In the contrivance of this there must be as many several characters as there are primitive words."

Hebrew is "the best pattern, because that language consists of fewest radicals." Each primitive needs marks for "cases, conjugations, or other necessary variations of those derivatives that depend upon it."

### Pronunciation Independence

> "In the reading of such a writing, though men of several countries should each of them differ in their voices, and pronouncing several words, yet the sense would be still the same."

The example: the astronomical sign ♉. A Jew calls it **שור**, a Greek **Ταυρον**, an Italian **toro**, a Frenchman **taureau**, a German **Stier**, an Englishman **a bull** — "though several nations may differ in the expression of things, yet they all agree in the same conceit of them."

### Scale: Seven or Eight Thousand Characters

> "The learning of this character will not be more difficult than the learning of any one language, because there needs not be more signs for the expression of things, than there is now for the expression of words. Amongst those in China and Japan, there is said to be about seven or eight thousand."

## The Grand Ambition

> "The perfecting of such an invention were the only way to unite the seventy-two languages of the first confusion; and therefore may very well deserve their endeavours who have both abilities and leisure for such kind of enquiries."

This is Wilkins launching his life's project — inviting himself and others to the task. The 1668 *Essay* would attempt to classify all knowledge into a hierarchical scheme of 40 genera, subdivided into differences and species, each with its own systematic character. Though it failed as a practical universal language, it influenced Linnaeus's taxonomy, Roget's Thesaurus, and modern classification systems.
