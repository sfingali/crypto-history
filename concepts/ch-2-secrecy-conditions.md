# Chapter 2: Conditions Requisite to Secrecy

This chapter establishes the **two fundamental criteria of good secrecy** and introduces the first major category of secret communication: **Cryptologia** — secrecy of speech through hidden meaning in the matter itself (metaphor, allegory, parable).

## The Two Conditions of True Secrecy

> "To the exactness of secrecy in any way of discourse, there are these two qualifications requisite."

1. **Difficulty of discovery**: The message must be "difficult to be unfolded, if it should be doubted of, or examined."

2. **Freedom from suspicion**: It must be "(if possible) altogether devoid of suspicion; for so far as it is liable to this, it may be said to come short in the very nature of secrecy."

These two conditions work together. If a message is merely hard to decode but obviously suspicious, "a man is more likely to be disappointed in his intentions, when his proceedings are mistrusted." The highest art is to hide a message so it doesn't look like a message at all.

Wilkins notes that few of his later examples satisfy both — they are "here specified, to shew what a man should aim at."

## The Three-Fold Taxonomy of Secret Discourse

Wilkins introduces his defining terminology:

| Greek Term | English Meaning |
|------------|----------------|
| **Cryptologia** | Secret speaking |
| **Cryptographia** | Secret writing |
| **Semaeologia** | Secret discoursing by signs/gestures |

The general art is **Cryptomeneses** — "private intimations" in the broadest sense.

## Cryptologia: Secrecy in the Matter of Speech

Cryptologia can operate at two levels: the **matter** (what is spoken about) and the **words** (how it is spoken). Chapter 2 deals with the former.

### Metaphors, Allegories, and Rhetorical Tropes

> "When the thing we would utter is so concealed under the expression of some other matter, that it is not of obvious conceit."

This covers "metaphors, allegories, and divers other tropes of oratory" — which belong to rhetoric when used for ornament, but to grammar (cryptology) when used for secrecy.

### Aenigmatical Learning of the Ancients

The ancient heathen, and even their gods, were "so much devoted" to obscure speaking, as shown by "the strange and frequent ambiguities of the oracles and sybils." The most profound philosophers were those best able at "affected obscurities."

### The Mysterious Fables

> "Under which the ancients did veil the secrets of their religion and philosophy, counting it a prophane thing to prostitute the hidden matters of either unto vulgar apprehension."

Wilkins quotes Macrobius: nature and the gods "would not themselves have hidden so many things from us, if they had intended them for common understandings." The Egyptian priests, Pythagoreans, Platonists, and "almost all other sects and professions" wrapped their learning in "obscure and mysterious expressions."

### Biblical Parables

Wilkins makes an important theological move: connecting this pagan tradition to Scripture.

> "To this general custom of those ages (we may guess) the Holy Ghost does allude, in the frequent parables both of the Old and New Testament."

He quotes Aquinas: *"Parabola est sermo similitudinarius, qui aliud dicit, aliud significat"* — a parable says one thing and means another. The disciples directly opposed parables to plain speaking (John 16:29).

Christ's purpose was secrecy: "those proud and perverse auditors, who would not apply themselves to the obedience of his doctrine, might not so much as understand it." He cites Matthew 13:10-11 — "to them it is not given to know the mysteries of the kingdom of God."

### The Parable as a Trap

Parables have a tactical function: "so to imply a secret argument, that the adversary might unawares be brought over to an acknowledgment and confession of the thing we would have." Examples:

- **Nathan to David** (2 Samuel 12) — the rich man's ewe lamb reveals David's guilt over Uriah
- **The prophet to Ahab** (1 Kings 20) — makes Ahab condemn himself for letting the king of Syria escape
- **Christ's parable of the vineyard** (Matthew 21) — forces the unbelieving Jews to acknowledge their own deserved judgment

Wilkins adds a secular example: an ancient orator who, when enemies proposed peace on condition of banishing the philosophers, told a tale of wolves proposing peace to sheep on condition of expelling their mastiff-dogs — "by this means better instructing them of the danger and madness there would be, in yielding to such a condition."

### Parables Have Persuasive Power

> "This way of teaching hath a great advantage above any other, by reason it hath much more power in exciting the fancy and affections. Plain arguments and moral precepts barely proposed, are more flat in their operation, not so lively and persuasive, as when they steal into a man's assent, under the covert of a parable."

### The Art Requires Natural Talent

> "To be expert in this particular, is not in every man's power; like poetry, it requires such a natural faculty as cannot be taught."

But insofar as rules exist, they belong to oratory: choose a subject with "proper analogy and resemblance to the chief business," and so "aptly contrive the several parts of the similitude" that they answer to the most consequential passages.

### A Digression on Jewish Parables

Wilkins notes the Talmudic tradition of parabolic teaching, and warns that "many of those horrid fables that are fathered upon them" arise from literal misreadings — citing a rabbi's tale of a lion whose roaring at 400 leagues shook down Rome's walls, which was a moral allegory about divine majesty, not a claim about actual lions.

## Key Quotes

> "To the exactness of secrecy in any way of discourse, there are these two qualifications requisite. 1. That it be difficult to be unfolded... 2. That it be (if possible) altogether devoid of suspicion."

> "The art of secret information in the general, as it includes all significatory signs, may be stiled cryptomeneses, or private intimations."

> "Parabola est sermo similitudinarius, qui aliud dicit, aliud significat."
