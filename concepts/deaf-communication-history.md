# Deaf Communication History

John Wilkins's [Chapter 14](concepts/ch-14-signs-gestures.md) of *Mercury* (1641) is a source text in the history of deaf communication and sign language studies. His treatment of **semaeologia** — the study of signs and gestures — represents one of the earliest systematic catalogues of non-verbal communication in English.

## Wilkins's Framework

Wilkins divides gestures into two categories:

1. **Ex congruo** — gestures that naturally resemble what they express (pointing, miming). These are universally intelligible because they draw on shared human experience.

2. **Ex placito** — gestures with meaning established "by use and mutual compact" (arbitrary signs). These require prior agreement between communicators — like a finger-signing alphabet.

## Historical References

Wilkins cites [the Venerable Bede](entities/bede.md)'s *De Loquela per Gestum Digitorum* — an early medieval system for representing numbers through finger positions. He also references classical sources on gesture use among the Romans and the Persians.

## The Suspicion Problem

Wilkins notes that while gesture is perhaps the oldest form of communication (infants use it before speech), it suffers from the same flaw as other "invented" methods: **it is inherently suspicious**. Someone making elaborate hand signals is visibly communicating something — even if an observer can't decode it.

## The Gallaudet Copy

The 1694 edition of *Mercury* digitised for this wiki is held by the **Gallaudet University Library** — the world's only university designed for deaf and hard-of-hearing students. Their preservation of this copy reflects the chapter's enduring relevance to deaf studies.
