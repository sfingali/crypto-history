# Mercury (1641) vs Essay (1668): The Seed and the Fruit

[John Wilkins](entities/john-wilkins.md) published two books that bookend his intellectual career. *Mercury: or the Secret and Swift Messenger* appeared in 1641, when Wilkins was 27 — a young chaplain writing on the eve of civil war. *An Essay towards a Real Character, and a Philosophical Language* appeared in 1668, when Wilkins was 54 — a bishop of the established church, a founder of the [Royal Society](entities/royal-society.md), and one of the most respected intellectuals in England. Between them lie 27 years, a revolution, a Restoration, and the transformation of a practical manual into a philosophical monument.

## Dimensions of Comparison

| Dimension | *Mercury* (1641) | *Essay* (1668) |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| **Full title** | *Mercury: or the Secret and Swift Messenger. Shewing how a Man may with Privacy and Speed communicate his Thoughts to a Friend at any distance* | *An Essay towards a Real Character, and a Philosophical Language* |
| **Central problem** | How to communicate secretly and swiftly | How to represent all knowledge in a universal symbolic system |
| **Scope** | Cryptography and secret communication in all forms — ciphers, steganography, signals, gestures, universal characters | A complete taxonomy of all human knowledge, expressed through a constructed language of symbolic characters |
| **Length** | ~200 pages (1641 edition), 20 chapters | ~600 pages (folio), with elaborate taxonomic tables, a dictionary, and a grammar |
| **[Chapter 13](concepts/ch-13-universal-character.md) role** | One chapter among twenty — the seed of a larger vision, occupying ~6 pages | The entire book — the fruit of 27 years of development |
| **Tone** | Practical, urgent, conversational — a handbook for "statesmen and soldiers" in a time of impending conflict | Systematic, institutional, confident — a monumental work published under Royal Society sponsorship and dedicated to the Society's president |
| **Historical context** | 1641: Charles I's personal rule collapsing; the Long Parliament convening; Civil War imminent. Urgency of secret communication is immediate and military. | 1668: Restoration established; Royal Society chartered (1662); Wilkins a bishop. Institutional confidence of a settled scientific establishment. |
| **Primary audience** | Practical men: soldiers, diplomats, merchants | Scholars, philosophers, the international Republic of Letters |
| **China as precedent** | Cited as proof-of-concept: the Chinese "common character" shows a universal writing system is possible | Studied in detail: Chinese characters analysed as a model for taxonomic classification (though ultimately rejected as insufficiently systematic) |
| **Musical precedent** | Musical notes cited as an existing universal character (Chapter 13) | Musical analogy dropped — the *Essay*'s system is taxonomic and hierarchical, not harmonic |
| **Theological framing** | The Fall of Adam and the curse of Babel provide the *problem* — secret communication is a partial remedy within a fallen world | The Real Character is a *solution* to Babel — an attempt to reverse the curse through rational-philosophical construction |
| **Publication context** | Published by John Maynard and John Wilkins, London | Published by Sa. Gellibrand and John Martyn, printers to the Royal Society; carries the Society's imprimatur |
| **Collaboration** | Sole-authored, though drawing on extensive sources | Collaborative: acknowledged contributions from John Ray (botany), Francis Willughby (zoology), William Lloyd (dictionary), and other Royal Society fellows |
| **Fate** | Multiple editions; influential in cryptographic literature; read by Pepys and Boyle | Monumental but impractical; admired but never adopted; Leibniz was fascinated; Borges mocked it; remains a landmark in the history of linguistics and artificial languages |

## The Seed: Chapter 13 of Mercury

[Chapter 13](concepts/ch-13-universal-character.md) of *Mercury* is a **manifesto** — six pages that outline an entire research programme. Its argument is breathtakingly compressed:

> "After the fall of Adam, there were two general curses inflicted on mankind: the one upon their labours, the other upon their language."

Against labour, we have "all those common arts and professions." Against language, we have only "the Latin tongue, and the other learned languages" — partial remedies at best.

The vision: "if there were such an universal character to express things and notions, as might be legible to all people and countries, so that men of several nations might with the same ease both write and read it, this invention would be a far greater advantage in this particular, and mightily conduce to the spreading and promoting of all arts and sciences."

The key insight — radical for 1641 — is that **learning languages consumes time that could be spent learning things**. "That great part of our time which is now required to the learning of words, might then be employed in the study of things."

Wilkins marshals four existing precedents as proof-of-concept:

1. **Chinese characters**: "Those of China and Japan do as much differ in their language as the Hebrew and the Dutch; yet either of them can, by this help of a common character, as well understand the books and letters of the others."

2. **Numbers**: "Many nations do agree in the characters of the common numbers" — Roman numerals and "Barbarian" (Arabic) figures.

3. **Astronomical and chemical signs**: Planets, aspects, minerals — all expressed in symbols transcending language.

4. **Musical notes**: "In most countries are the same: nor is there any reason why there may not be such a general kind of writing invented for the expression of every thing else."

The chapter ends with a call to arms: "The perfecting of such an invention were the only way to unite the seventy-two languages of the first confusion; and therefore may very well deserve their endeavours who have both abilities and leisure for such kind of enquiries."

This is not a plan. It is a **provocation** — and it provoked Wilkins himself for the next 27 years.

## The Fruit: The Essay of 1668

The *Essay* is what happens when a manifesto meets 27 years of work, institutional support, and collaborative scholarship. Its structure is monumental:

1. **A taxonomy of all knowledge**: Wilkins divides the universe into 40 genera (top-level categories), each subdivided into species under species. "World" (Genus I) contains "Celestial," "Elemental," "Terrestrial," etc. Every concept in human knowledge is assigned a place in this hierarchical tree.

2. **A philosophical language**: Each concept receives a written character (a symbol) and a spoken name (a word) derived systematically from its taxonomic position. Related concepts sound similar; unrelated concepts sound different. The system is generative: knowing the taxonomy, you can deduce the character for any concept.

3. **A "real character"**: The written symbols represent **things, not words**. A reader of any language could understand the meaning without knowing how to pronounce it — just as `II` (Taurus) means the same to a Jew reading *shor*, a Greek reading *tauros*, and an Englishman reading *bull*.

4. **A dictionary**: Organised by concepts, not alphabetically — a conceptual thesaurus keyed to the taxonomic system.

5. **A grammar**: Rules for how the philosophical language handles number, tense, case, and other grammatical categories.

The *Essay* is a work of staggering ambition and genuine intellectual achievement. It influenced Leibniz's *characteristica universalis*, Linnaeus's taxonomic system, Roget's *Thesaurus*, and the tradition of artificial languages from Esperanto to modern knowledge representation systems.

## What Changed in 27 Years

### 1. From Urgency to Confidence

The *Mercury* of 1641 is written in the shadow of war. The Civil War broke out in 1642, the year after publication. The book's practical tone — "here is how to send a secret message past enemy lines" — reflects a world where such knowledge might literally save lives.

The *Essay* of 1668 is written in the confidence of the Restoration. Wilkins is now Bishop of Chester, a pillar of the established church and the scientific establishment. The Royal Society has been chartered. The tone is no longer "you might need this tomorrow" but "this will advance human knowledge for centuries."

### 2. From Solitary Scholar to Institutional Leader

The 27-year-old Wilkins of 1641 cited his sources (Trithemius, Porta, Selenus, Bede, Josephus) but worked essentially alone. The 54-year-old Wilkins of 1668 could mobilise the Royal Society's collective expertise. John Ray handled the botanical taxonomy; Francis Willughby the zoological; William Lloyd compiled the dictionary. The *Essay* is the product of what Bacon had imagined: collaborative, institutional science.

### 3. From Chapter to Book

In *Mercury*, the universal character is **one idea among twenty** — a fascinating speculation appended to a book about ciphers. In the *Essay*, it is **the entire project**. Cryptography has been left behind. The *Essay* contains no cipher systems, no secret inks, no torch telegraphs. The universal character has outgrown its origins.

### 4. From Proof-of-Concept to System

The *Mercury* Chapter 13 essentially says: "Look, the Chinese do it, astronomers do it, musicians do it — clearly a universal character is possible." The *Essay* says: "Here is the system. Forty genera. Thousands of concepts. Every one assigned its place."

The move from possibility claim to working system is the difference between a manifesto and a monument.

## What the Essay Retained from Mercury

Despite 27 years of development, three core ideas from Chapter 13 survive in the *Essay*:

### 1. The Chinese Character Precedent

In *Mercury*: "Those of China and Japan do as much differ in their language as the Hebrew and the Dutch; yet either of them can, by this help of a common character, as well understand the books and letters of the others."

In the *Essay*: Chinese characters are analysed in detail. Wilkins acknowledges them as the only existing example of a "real character" but argues they are insufficiently systematic — characters for "a horse" and "a mare" share no visible relationship despite the conceptual connection. The *Essay* aims to do better: related concepts should have related characters, making the system learnable by principle rather than by rote memorisation of thousands of symbols.

### 2. The Picture Analogy

In *Mercury*: "As it is in the picture of a man, a horse, or tree; which to all nations do express the same conceit, though each of these creatures be stiled by several names, according to the difference of languages."

In the *Essay*: This becomes the founding principle. The real character is a "picture" of concepts — not a visual depiction but a **logical representation** that bypasses spoken language entirely.

### 3. The Musical Idea (Diminished)

In *Mercury*: Musical notes are cited as an existing universal character — "in most countries are the same."

In the *Essay*: The musical analogy is largely absent. Wilkins's mature system is hierarchical and taxonomic, not harmonic. The *Essay* does not map concepts to musical intervals. But the underlying insight — that a non-linguistic symbol system can be universally understood — survives in more sophisticated form.

## The String Not Followed: Secrecy

The most striking thing the *Essay* does **not** retain is secrecy. *Mercury* is a book about secret communication. The *Essay* is a book about universal communication — the opposite problem. Where *Mercury* asks "how do I hide my meaning from most people?", the *Essay* asks "how do I make my meaning accessible to all people?"

This is not a contradiction but a **completion**. Wilkins's intellectual arc moves from the problem of the few (secret communication between confederates) to the problem of the many (universal communication across nations). The 27-year journey transforms the cryptographer into the universal linguist.

## Synthesis and Verdict

*Mercury* and the *Essay* are not two books by the same author. They are two **instalments of one intellectual life**. The 27-year-old Wilkins of 1641 planted a seed in Chapter 13 — the possibility of a universal character that could transcend Babel. The 54-year-old Wilkins of 1668 harvested the fruit — a complete philosophical language, published under the auspices of the Royal Society, the most ambitious linguistic project of the 17th century.

The relationship is developmental, not competitive. *Mercury* is better as a book you can use — practical, vivid, encyclopaedic within its domain. The *Essay* is more important as an intellectual achievement — a landmark in the history of linguistics, taxonomy, and artificial languages. But without Chapter 13 of *Mercury*, the *Essay* might never have been conceived. And without the *Essay*, Chapter 13 would remain a provocative footnote — a brilliant suggestion that nobody followed up.

The seed and the fruit. The manifesto and the monument. The cryptographer and the universal linguist. They are the same man, 27 years apart.
